Lesson 1.06 · Friday Workshop
Predicting and Annotating as You Read
Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
⌚ ~10 min
Good readers do not just take in words. They guess what is coming and leave themselves notes along the way.
You will read Chapter 6 and practice two habits that strong readers use all the time: predicting, which means making a smart guess about what will happen next, and annotating, which means marking the text with quick notes as you go.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to make a prediction based on clues in the text, not just a wild guess.
2
I will be able to annotate Chapter 6 with a small set of marks that I can reuse all year.
3
I will be able to describe how Swift praises Lilliput’s laws while quietly showing its court is rotten.
📋 WHAT YOU NEED FOR THIS LESSON
▶
Gulliver’s Travels, Part I, Chapter 6. [Teacher: link the Chapter 6 PDF in Canvas Files here]
▶
Your Gulliver’s Travels Reading Journal on OneDrive.
💡 YOUR ANNOTATION KEY (FOUR MARKS)
You do not need a complicated system. These four marks cover almost everything. Use them in your Journal as you read.
✔️ Underline
an important detail or a line you want to remember.
❓ Question mark
next to something confusing or surprising.
💡 Lightbulb
where you think Swift is making a point about real people.
👉 Arrow
a quick prediction about what might happen next.
📚 PART 1 · READ & NOTICE: PREDICT FIRST

At the end of Chapter 5, the empress is furious with Gulliver and Flimnap the treasurer has started turning the emperor against him. A good prediction uses those clues.

⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Based on how Chapter 5 ended, what do you predict for Gulliver? (No wrong answer. A prediction is a guess, and you will check it as you read.)
📚 PART 2 · READ & NOTICE: READ CHAPTER 6, MARKING AS YOU GO

Read Chapter 6 now, using your four marks. Gulliver describes how everything in Lilliput is sized in exact proportion, then explains the country’s laws and customs. Some of these laws sound surprisingly wise: people who accuse others falsely are punished, those who keep the law are rewarded, and officials are chosen for good character more than for cleverness. Then Gulliver describes his own daily life, and the chapter ends with Flimnap the treasurer growing jealous and urging the emperor to get rid of Gulliver.

Notice the gap. Swift describes laws that reward honesty and good character, but the actual court runs on rope-dancing, flattery, and a jealous treasurer’s lies. Mark a lightbulb wherever you spot that gap.

⚡ TRY IT · WHICH MARK FITS?

For each moment from Chapter 6, choose the mark that fits best. Think first, then choose.

1. Lilliput’s laws reward people who keep the law, not just punish people who break it.
2. The Lilliputians bury their dead with their heads pointing straight down.
3. Flimnap tells the emperor that Gulliver costs too much and should be dismissed.
🎯 FRIDAY WORKSHOP · PUTTING IT TOGETHER

This week you practiced inference, point of view, and annotation. Today’s workshop turns those skills toward your own writing. In the Quick Write, you will exaggerate something small to make a point, exactly the move Swift makes with rope-dancing and egg wars.

✏️ READING JOURNAL · ADD THIS TO THE WEEK 2 SECTION

List two or three small things in everyday life that people treat far more seriously than they deserve, such as a tiny rule, an argument over a game, or a feud over a team. Star the one that would be easiest to exaggerate into a tiny war. You will use it to start your Quick Write.

💪 BEFORE YOU GO

Lilliput has wise laws on paper and a foolish court in practice. Swift wants you to feel that gap, not just read about it. A reader who predicts and annotates catches gaps like that everywhere.
