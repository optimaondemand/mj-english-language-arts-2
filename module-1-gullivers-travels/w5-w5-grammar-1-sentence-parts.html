Grammar 1.09
Sentence Parts: The Subject and the Predicate
Lesson
📝 TODAY'S FOCUS
⌚ ~12 min
Every complete sentence has two main parts. One part names who or what the sentence is about. The other part tells what that person or thing does or is. Today you will split sentences from Gulliver’s Travels into those two parts and learn to find the line between them.
📚 THIS WEEK'S SENTENCE (from Chapter 6)
“His majesty was only shaved twice a-week.”
⚡ WARM-UP
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Before the lesson, how sure are you that you can split a sentence into “who or what” and “what they do”? (No wrong answer.)
💡 THE ONE RULE TO REMEMBER
A complete sentence has a subject and a predicate. The subject is who or what the sentence is about. The predicate is everything that tells what the subject does or is. Draw a line between them and the whole sentence sorts itself in two.
SUBJECT NOUN · who or what?
His majesty
|
PREDICATE · what about the subject?
was only shaved twice a-week
The predicate is built around the verb. To find it, ask What is being said about the subject? That question works for an action verb and a linking verb alike. The predicate is the verb plus everything that travels with it.
📚 HOW TO SPLIT A SENTENCE: ASK TWO QUESTIONS

To split a sentence into its two parts, find the verb first, then ask who or what goes with it. Watch how it works on this week’s sentence.

  1. What is being said about his majesty?  was shaved. That is the verb, so the predicate is built around it.
  2. Who or what was shaved?  His majesty. The answer is the subject noun: who the sentence is about.
  3. What does the rest tell us?  only twice a-week tells how often, so it belongs with the verb in the predicate.
Label what you found:
His majesty
subject noun | was only shaved twice a-week.
predicate (verb: shaved)

One reminder: the subject is not always the first word. In “In this manner I had several conversations,” the subject is I, a subject pronoun, not the opening phrase. Always find the verb first by asking what is being said about the subject, then ask who or what.
