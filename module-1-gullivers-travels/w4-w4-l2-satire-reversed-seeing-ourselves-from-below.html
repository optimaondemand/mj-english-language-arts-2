Satire Reversed: Seeing Ourselves From Below

📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
In Lilliput, Swift shrank other people so we could laugh at their pride. In Brobdingnag, he shrinks Gulliver, who stands in for us. Now the joke is on humanity.
Today you read Chapter 3, where Gulliver is bought by the queen and meets the king. You will study how Swift reverses the satire to mock human pride from below, and how his vivid imagery makes you feel tiny right along with Gulliver.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to explain how Swift uses the giants to satirize human pride.
2
I will be able to describe what the king of Brobdingnag thinks of humankind after Gulliver brags about Europe.
3
I will be able to identify imagery (vivid word pictures) that makes a scene feel huge or tiny.

💡 THE ONE IDEA TO HOLD ONTO: WHO THE JOKE IS ON
PART I · LILLIPUT
We laugh AT the tiny people
Their wars over which end of an egg to crack make human politics look silly. The joke points outward.
➞
PART II · BROBDINGNAG
The giants laugh AT us
Gulliver is the tiny one now, so when the king mocks "diminutive insects," he is mocking humankind. The joke points back at the reader.
Satire = humor and exaggeration used to criticize. In Part II, the target is human pride itself.
📚 PART 1 · READ & NOTICE: BEFORE YOU BEGIN
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Gulliver is about to brag to a giant king about how great his home country is. How do you predict the king will react? (No wrong answer.)
📚 PART 2 · READ & NOTICE: NOW READ CHAPTER 3

From a farmer’s sideshow to the queen’s court

Read Part II, Chapter 3 now. The farmer works Gulliver so hard at markets that he nearly dies, so he sells him to the queen. At court Gulliver finds safety and comfort, and Glumdalclitch stays as his nurse. The king, a serious and learned man, at first thinks Gulliver might be a clever piece of clockwork. His scholars cannot decide what Gulliver is and finally label him a freak of nature.

The key moment comes when the king questions Gulliver about Europe. Gulliver proudly describes his country’s trade, its wars, and its politics. The king is not impressed. He picks Gulliver up, strokes him, and delivers his verdict on humankind:

📚 FROM THE TEXT (Part II, Chapter 3)
“...how contemptible a thing was human grandeur, which could be mimicked by such diminutive insects as I.”

To the king, human greatness is a small thing if a six-inch creature can act it out so completely. Then Gulliver admits something surprising. After living among the giants for months, he looks in a mirror beside the queen and starts to agree. He writes that he "really began to imagine myself dwindled many degrees below my usual size." The perspective flip has begun to change how Gulliver sees himself.

✏️ ADD THIS TO YOUR READING JOURNAL
In the Week 4 section of your Reading Journal, write two or three sentences: why does the king call human grandeur "contemptible," and what does Gulliver start to feel about his own size after living among the giants? Quote a few words from the chapter to back up your answer.
📚 PART 3 · READ & NOTICE: IMAGERY THAT MAKES YOU FEEL TINY

Imagery is language that paints a picture for your senses. Swift fills Part II with imagery that forces you to feel the size difference. When the queen eats, she "would craunch the wing of a lark, bones and all, between her teeth." You do not just read that she is big. You hear the crunch and feel small.

For each detail below, decide which sense it appeals to most. This is how you train your eye to notice imagery instead of skimming past it. Do each one in your head first.

⚡ TRY IT · WHICH SENSE DOES THE IMAGE USE?
Match each image from Chapter 3 to the sense it appeals to most strongly.
1. The queen "would craunch the wing of a lark, bones and all, between her teeth."
2. Through his spectacles, the dim-sighted scholar’s eyes "appeared like the full moon shining into a chamber at two windows."
3. The queen’s dwarf drops Gulliver into a "large silver bowl of cream," where he falls "over head and ears."
⚠️ WORTH KNOWING · USING AI TO LOOK THINGS UP
An AI chatbot can define imagery for you in one line. What it cannot do is feel the crunch of the queen’s teeth the way you can while reading, or decide which images in Chapter 3 hit you hardest and why. That sensing and judging is your work as a reader, and it is the skill you will keep needing long after the definition is easy to find.
