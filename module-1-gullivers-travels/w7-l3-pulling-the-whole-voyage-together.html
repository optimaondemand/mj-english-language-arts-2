📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A giant in Lilliput. A speck in Brobdingnag. A “Yahoo” among the wise horses. Four times Swift changed Gulliver’s size or his company, and four times he held up a mirror. What was the mirror always showing?
You will read Chapter 12, the end of the book, and pull all the voyages together. You will see how one idea, human pride, ties the whole journey into a single argument about people.

💡 THE ONE IDEA TO HOLD ONTO
Swift keeps changing Gulliver’s perspective, his size or his company, and each change exposes the same target: human pride. The voyages are not separate stories. They are four runs at one idea.
Voyage Gulliver’s position What the mirror shows
LilliputA giant among tiny peopleBig quarrels (which end to crack an egg) are silly up close.
BrobdingnagA speck before giantsA wise king sees England’s boasts as small and corrupt.
HouyhnhnmsA “Yahoo” among reasoning horsesHumans look like animals who use reason mostly to do harm.
📚 PART 1 · READ & NOTICE: READ CHAPTER 12

Read Chapter 12 now, the last chapter. Gulliver swears he has told the truth. He mocks the way explorers “discover” a peaceful land, plant a flag, and call it their king’s, which Swift uses to attack the pride of empires. Then comes the ending that pulls everything together. Gulliver lists the human types he can stand: lawyers, thieves, fools, politicians. He says he can tolerate all of them. But there is one thing he cannot bear:

“...when I behold a lump of deformity and diseases, both in body and mind, smitten with pride, it immediately breaks all the measures of my patience.”

The whole book has been building to this. The vice Swift attacks above all others is pride.

📚 PART 2 · READ & NOTICE: THE TRAP SWIFT SETS

Look closely at Gulliver in the final chapter. He believes he is now better than other people. He cannot sit near his family. He talks to horses for hours. He keeps his nose stopped against the smell of humans. Now weigh the irony.

1. Gulliver hates pride more than any other vice. Is Gulliver himself proud by the end of the book? Point to one detail from Chapter 12 to support your answer.
2. If a reader finishes the book sure that they are wiser than foolish Gulliver, how might they be falling into the very thing Swift warns against?

Add this to the Week 7 section of your Reading Journal.

⚡ TRY IT · HOW DOES EACH VOYAGE EXPOSE PRIDE?

For each detail below, place it on the scale: does it show pride being EXPOSED as foolish, or does it show genuine WISDOM that has no pride in it? Decide in your head first.

1. In Lilliput, two nations go to war over which end of an egg to crack first.
2. The Houyhnhnms “are no more proud of the good qualities they possess” than a person would boast of having a leg or an arm.
3. Gulliver refuses to sit near his family and believes he has risen above his own species.
🧠 FRIDAY WORKSHOP · GET READY FOR THE PROJECT

You have now read all the voyages this module covers. Your Module 1 project asks: which voyage best reveals Swift’s view of humanity? Before you write your Quick Write, take two minutes in your Reading Journal:

Jot one strong detail from Lilliput and one from Brobdingnag that show pride.
Decide, for now, which voyage you find the most convincing. You will defend that choice in your project.
