Parts of Speech Review: Eight Jobs Words Can Do

📝 TODAY'S FOCUS
You have learned eight parts of speech this module: noun, verb, adjective, adverb, preposition, conjunction, pronoun, and interjection. Each one is just a job a word can do in a sentence. Today you will pull them all together by classifying every word in one real sentence from Brobdingnag, then practice on new lines.
📚 THIS WEEK'S SENTENCE (Part II, Chapter 1, lightly trimmed)
“The huge farmer gently lifted me, and he carried me home.”
⚡ WARM-UP
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Eight parts of speech is a lot to track. Which one still feels trickiest to you? (No wrong answer. This tells you where to focus.)
💡 THE EIGHT JOBS, AT A GLANCE
Noun
names a person, place, thing, or idea (farmer, home). As the doer, it is the subject noun.
Verb
tells what is being said about the subject (lifted, carried). It can be an action verb or a linking verb.
Adjective
describes a noun: what kind? which one? how many? (huge). a, an, the are article adjectives.
Adverb
describes a verb: how? when? where? to what extent? (gently)
Preposition
shows a relationship and starts a phrase (in, after, under). The noun after it is the object of the preposition.
Conjunction
joins words or sentences (and, but, so)
Pronoun
replaces a noun (he, me, it). A doer pronoun is a subject pronoun.
Interjection
bursts out with feeling (Oh! Alas,)
📚 CLASSIFY THE WHOLE SENTENCE: ASK QUESTIONS, IN ORDER

Go word by word and ask the question that fits each job. The answers tell you what each word is doing. Watch the flow on this week’s sentence.

  1. What is being said about the farmer?  The farmer lifted (and later carried). Those are the verbs.
  2. Who or what lifted?  farmer. That is the subject noun. In the second half, the doer is he, which stands in for the farmer, so it is a subject pronoun.
  3. The huge: which one or what kind of farmer?  huge tells what kind, so it is an adjective. The is an article adjective.
  4. Lifted how?  gently. It tells how the action happened, so it is an adverb.
  5. What word joins the two halves of the sentence?  and. That is a conjunction.
  6. Carried me where?  home. It tells where, so here it works as an adverb. And me replaces Gulliver’s name, so it is a pronoun.

One word can do different jobs in different sentences. Home is a noun in "He carried me to his home," but it works as an adverb in "he carried me home." Always ask what the word is doing in this sentence.
