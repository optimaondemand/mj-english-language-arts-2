Adverbs: Words That Describe the Action
Lesson
📝 TODAY'S FOCUS
⌚ ~12 min
An adjective describes a noun. An adverb describes a verb. It tells you how, when, where, or to what extent something happens. Today you will find the adverbs in a sentence from Chapter 4, where a giant tries to walk through a tiny city without crushing it.
📚 THIS WEEK'S SENTENCE (from Chapter 4)
“I stepped over the great western gate, and passed very gently through the two principal streets.”
⚡ WARM-UP
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Last lesson you classified adjectives. Adverbs are their cousins. Before today, which one feels easier to spot? (No wrong answer.)
💡 THE ONE RULE TO REMEMBER
After you find the verb, ask it these four questions. Any word that answers one of them is doing an adverb’s job.
⚙️
How?
passed gently, walked slowly
⌛
When?
arrived later, came daily
📍
Where?
looked up, stepped here
🌡️
To what extent?
very gently, almost invisible
📚 HOW TO FIND AN ADVERB: ASK QUESTIONS ABOUT THE SENTENCE

Find the verb first. Then ask it how, when, where, or to what extent. Watch how it works on this week’s sentence.

  1. What is being said about I?  I passed. Passed is the verb.
  2. Who or what passed?  I. I is a pronoun, so it is the subject pronoun.
  3. Passed how?  gently. The answer tells how, so it is an adverb.
  4. How gently?  very. This word tells to what extent, so it is an adverb too. Here it describes the adverb gently.
Label what you found:
I
subject pronoun passed
verb very
adverb gently
adverb through the streets.

Two reminders: many adverbs end in -ly, like gently and quickly, but not all do. Words like very, now, here, and often are adverbs without the -ly. And an adverb can describe a verb, an adjective, or even another adverb, the way very describes gently here.
