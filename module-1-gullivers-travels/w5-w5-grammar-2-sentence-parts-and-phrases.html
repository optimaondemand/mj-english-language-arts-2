📝 TODAY'S FOCUS
⌚ ~12 min
Yesterday you split sentences into subject and predicate. Today you will look closer at the predicate and find phrases: small groups of words that work together as one unit. The most common kind starts with a preposition and ends with the object of the preposition, telling where or when. You will spot them in sentences from Gulliver’s Travels.
📚 THIS WEEK'S SENTENCE (from Chapter 8)
“I felt my box raised very high in the air.”
⚡ WARM-UP
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
A phrase is a small group of words that works as one unit, like “in the air.” How does that idea sit with you right now? (No wrong answer.)
💡 THE ONE RULE TO REMEMBER
A phrase is a group of words that go together but do not have their own subject and verb. A prepositional phrase begins with a preposition (a small place-or-time word like in, on, to, with) and ends with a noun, the object of the preposition. It often tells where or when.
Subject, predicate, and a phrase inside the predicate:
I
subject pronoun felt my box raised very high
predicate (verb: felt) in the air
prepositional phrase (where?)
The boxed phrase “in the air” works as one unit. It begins with the preposition in and ends with air, the object of the preposition, and it tells you where the box was raised.
📚 HOW TO FIND A PHRASE: ASK QUESTIONS ABOUT THE SENTENCE

Find the subject and verb first, then look for a small word that opens a phrase and ask what it answers. Watch how it works on this week’s sentence.

  1. What is being said about the sentence?  felt. That is the verb. Who or what felt?  I, a subject pronoun. The rest is the predicate.
  2. Is there a small place-or-time word?  in. A preposition often starts a phrase.
  3. In what?  the air. The preposition in plus air, the object of the preposition, makes the phrase “in the air,” and it tells where.

One reminder: a phrase is not a complete sentence. “In the air” cannot stand alone, because it has no subject and no verb of its own. It only adds information to the rest of the sentence.
