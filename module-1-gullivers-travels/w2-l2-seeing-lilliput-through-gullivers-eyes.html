Seeing Lilliput Through Gulliver’s Eyes
Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
⌚ ~12 min
Everything you know about Lilliput, you learned from one person: Gulliver. What if he is not telling you the whole story?
You will read Chapter 5, where Gulliver captures an entire enemy fleet. The story is told in the first person, which means one narrator chooses what to show you and how to describe it. Today you will learn how that single point of view shapes what you believe.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to explain what first-person point of view is and who tells this story.
2
I will be able to notice how Gulliver’s word choices nudge me toward a feeling about the people he describes.
3
I will be able to ask what the Blefuscudians might say if they told the same events.

💡 THE ONE IDEA TO HOLD ONTO
In a first-person story, the events never reach you directly. They pass through the narrator first. He picks which moments to tell, and which words to use, before you ever see them.
⚔️
What happened
Gulliver hooks and tows away fifty enemy warships.
➞
👤
Gulliver’s filter
He calls it a brave exploit and shouts “Long live the king!”
➞
👀
What you receive
You tend to feel the raid was heroic, because he framed it that way.
📚 PART 1 · READ & NOTICE: BEFORE YOU READ
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Think of a time you only heard one side of an argument between two friends. How much did you trust that one version? (No wrong answer.)
📚 PART 2 · READ & NOTICE: READ CHAPTER 5

Read Chapter 5 now. Gulliver wades and swims across the channel to Blefuscu, hooks fifty enemy warships with cables, and drags the whole fleet back to Lilliput. He protects his eyes from the enemy’s arrows with a pair of spectacles. The emperor makes him a nardac, the highest title in the land. But when the emperor wants Gulliver to help enslave all of Blefuscu, Gulliver refuses, saying he will never help bring “a free and brave people into slavery.” That refusal quietly turns the emperor against him.

As you read, notice the words Gulliver uses for himself and his actions, and the words he uses for the enemy. He is the only narrator. You are seeing Blefuscu entirely through the eyes of the man who just towed away their navy.

💡 A WORD FOR YOUR TOOLKIT
Point of view is the position from which a story is told. This whole book uses first person: the narrator is a character in the story and uses “I.” A first-person narrator can only tell you what he saw, felt, and chose to share.
⚡ TRY IT · FACT, OR GULLIVER’S SPIN?

Some of what Gulliver tells us is a plain fact almost anyone would report the same way. Other parts are his own slant: a feeling or judgment he adds. For each line, decide which it is. Think first, then choose.

1. “...with great ease drew fifty of the enemy’s largest men of war after me.”
2. Gulliver refuses to bring “a free and brave people into slavery.”
3. The Blefuscudians “set up such a scream of grief and despair” when their fleet moved.
4. The emperor “created me a nardac upon the spot, which is the highest title of honour among them.”
📚 PART 4 · READ & NOTICE: THE MISSING VOICE

Gulliver calls the fleet seizure a glorious adventure. The people of Blefuscu were minding their harbor when a giant waded over and stole their entire navy. Same event, very different feeling.

✏️ READING JOURNAL · ADD THIS TO THE WEEK 2 SECTION

Retell the fleet raid in two or three sentences the way a Blefuscudian sailor would, using the same facts Gulliver reports. Then add one line: which word choices in Chapter 5 show that you are seeing the raid through Gulliver’s eyes, not theirs?

➡️ WHAT YOU'LL DO NEXT

💪 BEFORE YOU GO

A first-person narrator is a window and a wall at the same time. He shows you a great deal, and he hides everything he did not choose to mention. All week, keep asking: who is telling me this, and what might they be leaving out?
