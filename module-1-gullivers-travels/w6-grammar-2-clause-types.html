Clause Types: Simple, Compound, Complex
Lesson
⏱ ~12 min lesson
📝 TODAY'S FOCUS
Yesterday you learned that clauses can be independent or dependent. Today you put them together. The way the clauses combine decides the type of sentence: simple, compound, or complex. You will name each type using sentences from Gulliver’s Travels.
📚 THIS WEEK'S SENTENCES (from Part IV)
“The two horses came up close to me.” (Ch. 1)
“The horse made me a sign to go in first, and I obeyed.” (adapted from Ch. 2)
“When the beast felt the smart, he drew back.” (Ch. 1)
⚡ WARM-UP
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Think back to yesterday. Can you tell an independent clause from a dependent one before we begin? (No wrong answer.)
💡 THE ONE RULE TO REMEMBER
Count the clauses and notice how they join. That tells you the type.
SIMPLE · one independent clause
The two horses came up close to me.
One subject-verb idea that stands alone. Nothing added.
COMPOUND · two independent clauses joined
The horse made me a sign to go in first, and I obeyed.
Two clauses that could each stand alone, joined by a conjunction like and, but, or or.
COMPLEX · one independent + one dependent
When the beast felt the smart, he drew back.
A dependent clause leaning on an independent one.
📚 HOW TO NAME THE TYPE: ASK THREE QUESTIONS

To name the sentence type, count the clauses, then check whether each one can stand alone. Watch how it works on the compound sentence above.

  1. How many clauses?  Two: “The horse made me a sign to go in first” and “I obeyed.”
  2. Can each one stand alone?  Yes. Both are complete sentences on their own. So both are independent.
  3. Two independent clauses joined by the conjunction ‘and’?  That makes it a compound sentence.
Label what you found:
The horse made me a sign to go in first,
independent clause and I obeyed.
independent clause
two independent clauses joined → compound

One reminder: do not confuse compound and complex. Compound joins two clauses that can each stand alone. Complex joins an independent clause to a dependent one that cannot.
