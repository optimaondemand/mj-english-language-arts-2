💡 THE ONE IDEA TO HOLD ONTO
Figurative language compares two things to help you picture an idea. Two common kinds:
Simile
Compares using like or as.
From the chapter: the eagle meant to drop the box “like a tortoise in a shell.”
Metaphor
Says one thing is another, with no like or as.
From the chapter: among the small sailors, Gulliver felt that he was “a giant.”
📚 PART 1 · READ & NOTICE: READ CHAPTER 8

Read Chapter 8 now. On a trip to the coast, Gulliver naps in his travelling-box while a page hunts for birds’ eggs. A giant eagle seizes the box by its ring, fights off other birds, and drops it into the sea. An English ship finds the floating box and rescues a confused Gulliver, who at first takes the normal-sized sailors for “pigmies.” Back home, he keeps treating his own family as tiny, ducking through his own door “like a goose under a gate.”

Watch how Swift uses comparisons to make the scale of everything land for the reader: the splash, the eagle, the way Gulliver sees ordinary people once his eyes are used to giants.

⚡ TAP TO REVEAL · WHAT IS THE COMPARISON DOING?

Each card shows a line from Chapter 8. Read it, decide in your head what the comparison helps you picture, then tap to check.

⚡ TAP TO REVEAL
“...some eagle had got the ring of my box in his beak, with an intent to let it fall on a rock, like a tortoise in a shell...”  (Tap to see what this shows.)
A simile (it uses like). It shows Gulliver as small and helpless prey: the eagle treats his box exactly the way it would crack open a tortoise to eat it.
⚡ TAP TO REVEAL
“My fall was stopped by a terrible squash, that sounded louder to my ears than the cataract of Niagara...”  (Tap to see what this shows.)
A comparison using than, close to a simile. It makes the splash feel huge by measuring it against the loudest waterfall Gulliver can name.
⚡ TAP TO REVEAL
“I looked down upon the servants... as if they had been pigmies and I a giant.”  (Tap to see what this shows.)
A metaphor wrapped in a comparison: Gulliver casts himself as a giant and everyone else as tiny. His eyes are still stuck in Brobdingnag, so perspective, not real size, has changed.
⚡ TRY IT · SIMILE OR METAPHOR?

Each line below comes from Chapter 8. Decide whether the comparison is a simile (uses like or as) or a metaphor (says one thing is another). Decide in your head before you choose.

1. “...I bent down to go in, like a goose under a gate...”
2. Gulliver describes himself returning home as a giant among pigmies.
3. “...my box was tossed up and down, like a sign in a windy day.”
4. Gulliver calls the rescuing sailors “the most little contemptible creatures” and takes them for pigmies.

✏️ FRIDAY WORKSHOP · START YOUR PROJECT ARGUMENT

You have now seen both voyages: tiny Lilliput and giant Brobdingnag. The Module 1 project will ask which voyage better reveals Swift’s view of humanity. Today you write the first draft of that argument in Quick Write 5: a thesis statement plus one reason. Keep it; you will build on it.

A thesis is one sentence that states your claim. Example shape: “The voyage to ______ better reveals Swift’s view of humanity because ______.”
