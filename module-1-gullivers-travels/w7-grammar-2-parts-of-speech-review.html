📝 TODAY'S FOCUS
This module you have learned nouns, pronouns, verbs, adjectives, adverbs, prepositions, conjunctions, and interjections. Today you will pull them all together. You will take one full sentence from Gulliver’s Travels and name the job of every word in it.
📚 THIS WEEK'S SENTENCE (from Chapter 10)
“I had worked two chairs with my knife.”
⚡ WARM-UP
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
After several weeks of grammar, how confident are you about naming the job of any word in a sentence? (No wrong answer.)
💡 THE QUESTION FOR EACH JOB
Each part of speech has one plain question that finds it. Ask the question, and the word answers. Find the verb first, then the subject, then the rest.
VerbShows action or links the subject to a word that renames or describes it. Find it by asking "What is being said about the subject?" An action verb shows action; a linking verb joins; a helping verb works with a main verb. (had worked, was)
Subject noun or subject pronounThe naming word that the sentence is about. Find it by asking "Who or what" plus the verb. A subject noun is a noun; a subject pronoun is a pronoun like I, he, they. (I, Gulliver)
NounNames a person, place, thing, or idea. (knife, chairs)
PronounTakes the place of a noun. A possessive pronoun shows ownership. (I, he, them; my, his)
AdjectiveDescribes a noun. Find it by asking "What kind? Which one? How many?" (two, great)
Article adjectiveThe words a, an, and the, which point to a noun. (a, an, the)
AdverbDescribes a verb, an adjective, or another adverb. Find it by asking "How? When? Where? To what extent?" (gently, often)
PrepositionShows a relationship and starts a phrase that ends in a noun. That noun is the object of the preposition. (with, into)
ConjunctionJoins words or groups of words. (and, but, or)
InterjectionShows sudden feeling and stands apart from the rest of the sentence. (Oh, Alas)
📚 WORK THROUGH THE SENTENCE, ONE WORD AT A TIME

Watch how each question names a word in “I had worked two chairs with my knife.” Always find the verb first, then the subject, then the rest.

  1. What is being said about the subject?  had worked. That is the verb.
  2. Who or what had worked?  I. It takes the place of a name, so it is a pronoun, and it is the subject. We call it the subject pronoun.
  3. Had worked what?  chairs. It names a thing, so it is a noun.
  4. How many chairs?  two. It answers a "How many?" question, so it describes the noun and is an adjective.
  5. What shows a relationship and begins a phrase?  with. It begins a phrase that ends in the noun knife, so it is a preposition. Knife is the object of the preposition.
  6. Whose knife?  my. It points to the noun and shows ownership, so it is a possessive pronoun.
Label what you found:

Every word has a job. Once you name the verb and the subject, the rest fall into place fast.
