Conjunctions: The Words That Join
Lesson
⏱ ~12 min lesson
📝 TODAY'S FOCUS
A conjunction is a joining word. It links two words, two groups of words, or two whole ideas. The most common ones are and, but, and or. Today you will find the conjunctions in a sentence from Chapter 8 and learn a plain question that catches them. Swift leans on conjunctions to stack action on action as Gulliver makes his escape.
📚 THIS WEEK'S SENTENCE (from Chapter 8)
“Gulliver mended the boat and gathered his oars, but the wind rose.”
A shortened version of Swift’s account of Gulliver finding and fixing the overturned boat to leave Blefuscu.
⚡ WARM-UP
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Read the sentence above out loud. Which little word hooks two opposite ideas together, the good news and the bad? (No wrong answer.)
💡 THE ONE RULE TO REMEMBER
A conjunction sits between two things and joins them. The three most common joining words do three different jobs.
➕
and = adds
It puts two things together: oars and sails, mended and gathered.
♻
but = contrasts
It shows a turn or surprise: the boat was ready, but the wind rose.
⬆️
or = gives a choice
It offers options: stay in Lilliput or flee to Blefuscu.
📚 HOW TO FIND A CONJUNCTION: ASK QUESTIONS ABOUT THE SENTENCE

Work the sentence one piece at a time. Watch how it goes on this week’s line.

  1. Who or what is this sentence about?  Gulliver. That noun is the subject noun.
  2. What is being said about Gulliver?  mended. That is the verb (an action verb).
  3. Is there a second verb for the same subject?  gathered. Yes, a second verb.
  4. What word joins the two verbs?  and. It links mended and gathered, so it is a conjunction.
  5. Is a new, opposite idea added next?  Yes: the wind rose. What word joins it to the rest and shows the turn?  but. It is a conjunction too.
Label what you found:
Gulliver
subject noun mended
verb the boat
noun and
conjunction gathered
verb his oars,
noun but
conjunction the wind
subject noun rose.
verb

Notice that and joined two verbs, while but joined two complete ideas. A conjunction can link small parts or whole clauses. Its job is always the same: it joins.
