Lesson 1.19
The New Gulliver: A Character Who Changes
Lesson
~14 min
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
The Gulliver who sailed to Lilliput was proud of England and pleased with himself. The Gulliver who comes home cannot stand to be in the same room as his own family. What happened to him?
You will read Chapters 10 and 11. You will learn the difference between a dynamic character (one who changes) and a static character (one who stays the same), and you will trace exactly how Gulliver changed.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to define dynamic and static characters and tell them apart.
2
I will be able to name specific ways Gulliver has changed by the end of the voyage, using details from the text.
3
I will be able to judge whether Gulliver’s change makes him wiser or whether it has damaged him.

💡 THE ONE IDEA TO HOLD ONTO
A dynamic character changes across a story. A static character stays the same. Gulliver is dynamic: the man who comes home is not the man who left. Here is the same person, before and after.
GULLIVER AT THE START · PART I
Proud of England. Eager to please. Curious and cheerful. Glad to be among other people. Loves his family and longs for home.
➞
GULLIVER AT THE END · PART IV
Ashamed of being human. Cannot bear the sight of people. Faints when his wife hugs him. Prefers his horses, talking to them four hours a day.
That is a huge change. The question is whether it is growth or damage.
📚 PART 1 · TWO WORDS TO KNOW

Decide what each word means in your head, then tap to check.

⚡ TAP TO REVEAL
Dynamic character (tap to reveal)
A character who changes in an important way across the story. Their beliefs, feelings, or behavior are different at the end. Gulliver is dynamic.
⚡ TAP TO REVEAL
Static character (tap to reveal)
A character who stays essentially the same from beginning to end. The wise horse, Gulliver’s master, is static: he is calm and reasonable throughout.
📚 PART 2 · READ & NOTICE: READ CHAPTERS 10 AND 11

Read both chapters now. In Chapter 10 Gulliver is happy among the horses. He admires their honesty and calm so much that he starts to copy them: he trots like a horse and falls into their way of speaking. He begins to hate the sight of his own reflection. Then his master tells him the assembly has decided he must leave. Gulliver faints from grief, then builds a canoe to sail away.

In Chapter 11 he is rescued by a kind Portuguese captain, Don Pedro de Mendez, who treats him with patience and generosity. But Gulliver can barely stand to be near him. When he finally reaches home, his family is overjoyed; he is filled with disgust. He buys two horses and spends hours talking to them in the stable.

💡 KEEP THIS IN MIND
To prove a character is dynamic, point to the exact behaviors that changed. “He changed” is not enough. “He used to long for home, and now he faints when his wife hugs him” is evidence.
⚡ TRY IT · WHAT DOES THIS DETAIL SHOW?

Each detail below is from Chapter 10 or 11. For each one, choose what it shows about the new Gulliver. Decide in your head before you click.

1. When Gulliver sees his reflection in a lake, he “turned away... in horror and detestation of myself.”
2. Gulliver calls the kind captain a “Yahoo” and is “ready to faint at the very smell of him.”
3. When his wife hugs him on his return, Gulliver “fell into a swoon for almost an hour.”
4. Back home, Gulliver buys two horses and “converse[s] with them at least four hours every day.”
📚 PART 4 · READ & NOTICE: GROWTH OR DAMAGE?

A dynamic character changes, but the change is not always for the better. Gulliver believes he has become wiser. The people around him think he has lost his mind. Swift leaves this for you to judge, and good readers disagree. One thing to weigh: the kind captain, Don Pedro, treats Gulliver far better than Gulliver treats him.

1. Is the new Gulliver wiser, or damaged? State your judgment and back it with one specific detail from Chapter 10 or 11.
2. Name two specific behaviors that prove Gulliver is a dynamic character. For each, say what he used to be like and what he is like now.

Add this to the Week 7 section of your Reading Journal.
