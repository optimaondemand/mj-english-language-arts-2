Clause Types and Purposes: What a Sentence Is Doing
Lesson

📝 TODAY'S FOCUS
A clause is a group of words with a subject and a verb. A complete sentence is built from at least one clause. But sentences do different jobs: some tell, some ask, some command, some exclaim. Today you will sort sentences from Gulliver’s Travels by the job each one does.
📚 THIS WEEK'S SENTENCES (from Part IV)
“Take care of thyself, gentle Yahoo.” (Ch. 11)
“Whether the Yahoos should be exterminated from the face of the earth?” (Ch. 9)
⚡ WARM-UP
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
When you read a sentence out loud, do you hear the difference between a statement and a question? (No wrong answer.)
💡 THE ONE RULE TO REMEMBER
Every complete sentence does one of four jobs. The job is its purpose. The end punctuation almost always tells you which job it is.
TELLS · ends with .
Declarative
Makes a statement. “I finished a sort of Indian canoe.”
ASKS · ends with ?
Interrogative
Asks a question. “What country I was of?”
COMMANDS · ends with . or !
Imperative
Gives an order or request. “Take care of thyself.”
EXCLAIMS · ends with !
Exclamatory
Shows strong feeling. “What a wretched life!”
📚 HOW TO NAME A SENTENCE’S PURPOSE: ASK THREE QUESTIONS

To name the purpose of a sentence, ask the questions in order and stop at the first “yes.” Watch how it works on the sorrel nag’s farewell to Gulliver.

  1. Does it ask something?  No. There is no question. So it is not interrogative.
  2. Does it tell someone to do something?  Yes. It says “Take care of thyself.” The subject “you” is understood. So it gives a command or request. It is imperative.
  3. What does the end mark confirm?  A period. An imperative can end with a period or, with strong feeling, an exclamation point. The period fits a gentle goodbye.
Label what you found:
Take care of thyself, gentle Yahoo.
imperative · gives a request · understood subject “you”

One reminder: an imperative often hides its subject. “Take care of thyself” really means “[You] take care of thyself.” The subject “you” is understood, even though the word is not printed.
