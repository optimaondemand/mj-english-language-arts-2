📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
In Lilliput, Gulliver could hold an entire navy in one hand. Now a farmer picks him up between two fingers, the way you might pick up an insect. What changes when the giant becomes the small one?
Today you begin Part II, a new voyage to the land of giants called Brobdingnag. You will read Chapters 1 and 2 and study how Swift flips the scale, and how being small changes both how Gulliver sees the world and how the world sees him.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to describe how Gulliver reaches Brobdingnag and how the inhabitants first treat him.
2
I will be able to explain how the change in scale flips Gulliver’s point of view from Part I.
3
I will be able to explain what Gulliver realizes about size when he compares himself to a single Lilliputian.

💡 THE ONE IDEA TO HOLD ONTO: THE SCALE FLIPS
🧑
In Lilliput, Gulliver looked DOWN
A giant among thumb-sized people. He saw their pride from above and found it small.
⇆
👈
In Brobdingnag, Gulliver looks UP
A tiny man among giants. Now grass is twenty feet tall and a cat seems three times the size of an ox.
Same Gulliver, opposite position. The voyage is built so we feel both sides of the size difference.

📚 PART 2 · READ & NOTICE: HOW GULLIVER LANDS IN A LAND OF GIANTS

A second voyage, a second strange world

Read Part II, Chapters 1 and 2 now. Two months after returning home, Gulliver goes back to sea aboard the Adventure. A storm carries the ship far off course, and the crew lands on an unknown coast to look for fresh water. Gulliver wanders off alone. When he turns back, he sees a huge creature wading after the boat, and the crew rowing for their lives. He runs and hides in a field where the grass stands about twenty feet high and the grain rises forty feet.

A farmer as tall as a church steeple finds him. The farmer is careful, not cruel: he lifts Gulliver "between his fore-finger and thumb," studies him, and carries him home, where the family treats him with curiosity. In Chapter 2 the farmer’s nine-year-old daughter becomes Gulliver’s protector. She makes him clothes, teaches him the language, and gives him the name Grildrig. He calls her Glumdalclitch, or "little nurse." But the farmer also sees money in him and begins showing him off at markets like a sideshow curiosity.

💡 A NAME WORTH KNOWING
Glumdalclitch is the farmer’s daughter, "not above forty feet high, being little for her age." To her, Gulliver is a fragile pet she adores. Hold onto her: she will travel with Gulliver through all of Part II.
📚 PART 3 · READ & NOTICE: WHAT GULLIVER REALIZES ABOUT SIZE

Lying terrified in the giant grain field, Gulliver thinks back to Lilliput, where the people "looked upon me as the greatest prodigy that ever appeared in the world." Now he expects to be as unimportant here as a single Lilliputian was back among full-sized humans. That memory leads him to one of the most important sentences in the whole book:

📚 FROM THE TEXT (Part II, Chapter 1)
“Undoubtedly philosophers are in the right, when they tell us that nothing is great or little otherwise than by comparison.”

That single line is the engine of the whole voyage. Gulliver was not "big" in any final way. He was big only next to the Lilliputians, and small only next to the Brobdingnagians. Big and small depend on what you stand beside.

How the flip changes what Gulliver notices

Now that you have read Chapters 1 and 2, decide which details show the perspective flip at work, where being tiny makes Gulliver see ordinary things in a brand new way. Do each one in your head first, then choose.

⚡ TRY IT · DOES THIS SHOW THE PERSPECTIVE FLIP?
For each detail from Chapters 1 and 2, decide whether it shows the world looking different because Gulliver is now tiny, or is just background information about the voyage.
1. The farmer’s cat "seemed to be three times larger than an ox," and its fierce face fills Gulliver with fear.
2. A storm drives the ship far off course, and the crew lands to search for fresh water.
3. Gulliver struggles through a field where the grain rises "at least forty feet" and a hedge towers over a hundred feet high.
4. At dinner, the family places Gulliver on a table "thirty feet high from the floor," and he keeps far from the edge for fear of falling.
✏️ ADD THIS TO YOUR READING JOURNAL

Open the Week 4 section of your Reading Journal and answer these three questions in 2 to 4 complete sentences each. Use specific details from Chapters 1 and 2, not general guesses.

  1. Describe how Gulliver ends up among the giants. What does the farmer do when he first finds him, and how is that different from how the Lilliputians treated Gulliver?
  2. Find one ordinary thing that becomes dangerous or strange because Gulliver is now tiny (for example, the grain field, the cat, the dinner table, or the rats). Describe it and explain how the change in size makes it feel different.
  3. Gulliver says that "nothing is great or little otherwise than by comparison." In your own words, what does he mean, and how does Part II show it to be true?
