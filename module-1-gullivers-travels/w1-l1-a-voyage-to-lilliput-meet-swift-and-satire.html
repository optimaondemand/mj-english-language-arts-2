Lesson 1.01
A Voyage to Lilliput: Meet Jonathan Swift and the Art of Satire
Lesson ≈14 min
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A shipwrecked traveler wakes up tied to the ground by people six inches tall. Why would an author invent a world like that?
Today you meet Lemuel Gulliver and the writer who created him, Jonathan Swift. You will read Chapter 1 of Gulliver’s Travels and learn how Swift uses an invented world to say something true about the real one. That tool has a name: satire.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to describe who Jonathan Swift was and when he wrote Gulliver’s Travels.
2
I will be able to explain what satire is and how exaggeration can make a serious point.
3
I will be able to describe Gulliver’s first encounter with the Lilliputians in Chapter 1.

💡 THE ONE IDEA TO HOLD ONTO
🔭
1. Exaggerate
Blow something up to a ridiculous size, like a whole nation of six-inch people.
➞
🪴
2. Hold up a mirror
The silly world looks a lot like our own when you look closely.
➞
🎯
3. Make a point
The reader laughs, then realizes the author is criticizing something real.
Satire = using humor and exaggeration to criticize people, institutions, or ideas.

📚 PART 2 · READ & NOTICE: WHO WAS JONATHAN SWIFT?

A writer who used laughter as a weapon

Jonathan Swift was born in Dublin, Ireland, in 1667. He became a writer, a poet, and a cleric, eventually serving as Dean of St. Patrick’s Cathedral in Dublin. He published Gulliver’s Travels in 1726, and it has been read ever since as one of the great works of English literature. Swift was a master of satire. He used humor and irony to criticize the politics, the institutions, and the human pride of his time.

The novel follows Lemuel Gulliver, a curious and well-educated ship’s surgeon, through four strange voyages. In each one the size of the people around him changes, and each change forces Gulliver, and the reader, to look at human nature from a new angle. This year you will travel with him to three of those lands: Lilliput, Brobdingnag, and the country of the Houyhnhnms.

💡 DID YOU KNOW?
Swift lived during the Enlightenment, a time when European thinkers prized reason and science and questioned old authorities. Gulliver’s Travels reads like a wild adventure story, but Swift built it to poke at the politics and pride he saw all around him.
📚 PART 3 · READ & NOTICE: NOW READ CHAPTER 1

Read Part I, Chapter 1 now. Gulliver tells how he came from a small family in Nottinghamshire, trained as a surgeon, and went to sea. His ship, the Antelope, is wrecked in a storm. He swims to shore, falls asleep on the grass, and wakes to find he cannot move: an entire population of people six inches tall has tied him to the ground with hundreds of tiny strings. Watch how the Lilliputians treat this giant who has washed up on their beach.

✏️ READING JOURNAL · WEEK 1
Open the Week 1 section of your Reading Journal (the Word document in your OneDrive course folder) and answer in 2 to 4 sentences: How do the Lilliputians treat Gulliver after they capture him? Name two specific things they do, and say what those details show about them. Your teacher reviews the Journal at the Reading Journal Check, not every week.

Spotting satire in action

Now that you have read the chapter and know what satire is, decide which moments are Swift the satirist at work. Satire is not just anything strange or funny. It is a strange or funny thing that quietly criticizes how real people behave.

⚡ TRY IT · IS THIS SATIRE AT WORK?
For each detail from Chapter 1, decide whether Swift is mainly using it to gently mock human behavior, or simply moving the plot along. Do it in your head first, then choose.
1. The Lilliputians build an enormous machine with 22 wheels and 900 men to move Gulliver to the city.
2. Gulliver’s ship is wrecked in a storm and he swims to shore.
3. The six-inch emperor is described with grand titles and a court that guards his dignity carefully.
⚠️ WORTH KNOWING · USING AI TO LOOK THINGS UP
An AI chatbot can give you a quick summary of Swift’s life or a definition of satire in seconds, and that can be a useful starting point. It cannot notice for you which exact moments in Chapter 1 feel like satire and explain why. That noticing is your work as a reader, and it is the part that builds your judgment. This is true now, and it will still be true when you use these tools for bigger work.
