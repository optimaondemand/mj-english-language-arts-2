Lesson 1.13
The King’s Judgment and the Theme of Pride
Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
⌚ ~12 min
Gulliver spends hours describing England to the king of Brobdingnag, hoping the giant will admire it. The king listens carefully, then says something that lands like a slap. Why does Swift set it up this way?
You will read Chapter 6. You will follow how a single idea, called a theme, runs through the chapter: the idea of human pride, and how small that pride looks when a wiser observer examines it up close.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to explain what a theme is: an idea about life that an author builds across a whole story.
2
I will be able to describe what the king of Brobdingnag concludes about humankind, and trace that conclusion back to Gulliver’s own words.
3
I will be able to explain how Swift uses pride as the theme of this part of the book.

💡 THE ONE IDEA TO HOLD ONTO
A theme is one big idea an author returns to again and again. The theme here is pride. Watch what happens when Gulliver praises his country to someone wise enough to ask the hard questions. The bigger his boast, the smaller it looks. The king becomes a mirror.
WHAT GULLIVER SAYS · THE BOAST
England is glorious. Its parliament is the “most august assembly in Europe.” Its judges are wise, its armies brave, its history grand.
➞
WHAT THE KING CONCLUDES · THE MIRROR
The bulk of Gulliver’s countrymen are “the most pernicious race of little odious vermin that nature ever suffered to crawl upon the surface of the earth.”
The harder Gulliver works to make England sound proud and great, the worse it sounds to the king. That gap is where Swift puts the theme of pride.
📚 PART 1 · READ & NOTICE: BEFORE YOU READ
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
When someone brags hard about a place or a team they love, how do you usually react? (No wrong answer. Just check in with yourself.)
📚 PART 2 · READ & NOTICE: READ CHAPTER 6

Read Chapter 6 now. Gulliver, tiny in a land of giants, makes himself useful: he builds a comb from the king’s beard stumps, weaves chairs from the queen’s hair, and pounds out a jig on a sixty-foot spinet. Then the king sets him on the cabinet and they talk. Gulliver gives a long, glowing account of England: its parliament, its courts of justice, its treasury, its armies, its history.

The king does not argue. He asks questions. How do men become lords? Are judges ever bribed? Why do people spend fortunes to win a seat that pays nothing? Why keep a standing army in peacetime? After many hours, he gathers his notes, takes Gulliver gently into his hand, and delivers his verdict.

💡 KEEP THIS IN MIND
A theme is not the same as the plot. The plot is what happens: Gulliver talks, the king asks, the king judges. The theme is the idea underneath it: that pride can blind a person to the truth about themselves and their country.
⚡ TRY IT · WHAT IS THE KING REALLY ASKING?

The king asks polite questions, but each one points at a flaw Gulliver did not mean to reveal. For each question below, choose what the king is quietly suggesting. Decide in your head before you choose.

1. The king asks whether a lord was ever made noble because of “a sum of money to a court lady” rather than merit.
2. The king wonders why people spend fortunes, “often to the ruin of their families,” to win a seat in the Commons that pays no salary.
3. The king is “amazed” to hear of a paid standing army kept in peacetime among a free people.
4. The king notes that England’s spending is often double its taxes, and asks how “a kingdom could run out of its estate, like a private person.”
📚 PART 4 · READ & NOTICE: THE KING’S JUDGMENT

At the end of the chapter the king gives his verdict. He calls Gulliver’s account “a most admirable panegyric” (a speech of high praise) and then says it proves the opposite of what Gulliver hoped. He concludes that the bulk of Gulliver’s countrymen are “the most pernicious race of little odious vermin that nature ever suffered to crawl upon the surface of the earth.”

✏️ READING JOURNAL · ADD THIS TO THE WEEK 5 SECTION OF YOUR READING JOURNAL
Answer these in 2 to 4 sentences each. Point to a detail from Chapter 6, then explain your thinking:
  1. Pick one of the king’s questions about England (bought seats, bribed judges, or the standing army). What flaw in England does that question quietly expose?
  2. The king calls Gulliver’s countrymen “the most pernicious race of little odious vermin.” Using what Gulliver actually told him, why does the king reach this verdict?
  3. State the theme of this chapter in your own words, then name one detail that supports it.

💪 BEFORE YOU GO

Swift puts a proud man in front of a wiser one and lets pride do its own damage. As you finish Brobdingnag, keep asking the theme question: where does pride keep a character from seeing the truth?
