Clauses: Ones That Stand Alone, Ones That Lean
Lesson
⏱ ~12 min lesson
📝 TODAY'S FOCUS
A clause is a group of words that has its own subject and its own verb. Some clauses can stand alone as a sentence. Others cannot. Today you will learn to tell the two apart, using sentences from Gulliver’s Travels.
📚 THIS WEEK'S SENTENCE (from Part IV, Chapter 1)
“When I was a little refreshed, I went up into the country.”
⚡ WARM-UP
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Read this aloud: “When I was a little refreshed.” Does it feel finished, or does it leave you waiting for more? (No wrong answer.)
💡 THE ONE RULE TO REMEMBER
Every clause has a subject and a verb. The difference is whether it can stand on its own. An independent clause can be a sentence by itself. A dependent clause has a subject and a verb too, but it begins with a word that makes it lean on the rest of the sentence, so it cannot stand alone.
INDEPENDENT · stands alone
I went up into the country.
Subject pronoun: I. Verb: went. It is complete. You could stop here.
≠
DEPENDENT · leans on the rest
When I was a little refreshed…
Subject pronoun: I. Verb: was. But “When” leaves it hanging. It cannot stand alone.
A small set of opening words turn a clause dependent: when, because, if, although, since, while, after, before, until, as. When one of these starts a clause, it must join an independent clause to make a complete sentence.
📚 HOW TO TELL THEM APART: ASK THREE QUESTIONS

To label each clause, find the subject and verb, then ask whether it can stand alone. Watch how it works on this week’s sentence. Take it one clause at a time.

  1. First clause. What is the verb? Ask: what is being said about this group of words?  was. Who or what was?  I, the subject pronoun. So it has a subject and a verb. Can it stand alone?  No. “When” makes it lean, so it is dependent.
  2. Second clause. What is the verb? What is being said?  went. Who or what went?  I, the subject pronoun. Can it stand alone?  Yes. “I went up into the country” is a complete sentence, so it is independent.
  3. How do they join?  The dependent clause leans on the independent one. Together they make one complete sentence.
Label what you found:
When I was a little refreshed,
dependent clause + I went up into the country.
independent clause

One reminder: a dependent clause is not the same as a phrase. “In the country” is a phrase. It has no subject and no verb. “When I was a little refreshed” is a clause. It does have a subject and a verb. It just cannot stand alone.
