📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
⌚ ~12 min
Gulliver offers the king the secret of gunpowder, a gift that would make him “absolute master” of his people. The king refuses, and would rather lose half his kingdom than know it. Why?
You will read Chapter 7. You will compare two ways of running a country, England’s and Brobdingnag’s, and decide what Swift wants us to notice about each.

💡 THE ONE IDEA TO HOLD ONTO · TWO WAYS TO RUN A COUNTRY
Question England (as Gulliver tells it) Brobdingnag (the king’s way)
What makes government work?Thousands of books on the “art of government,” mystery, and intrigue.“Common sense and reason, justice and lenity.” Laws kept short and plain.
How long are the laws?Long, with comments and many readings, argued by paid lawyers.No law may be longer than the 22-letter alphabet. Writing a comment on a law is a crime.
What is the most useful thing a person can do?Win power, fame, and victories in war.Make “two ears of corn... grow... where only one grew before.”
Brobdingnag is not perfect. Its learning is limited and its king has blind spots. But Swift uses the side-by-side to ask a real question: what if simpler and plainer were also wiser?
📚 PART 1 · READ & NOTICE: READ CHAPTER 7

Read Chapter 7 now. Stung by the king’s judgment, Gulliver tries to win him back. He offers the secret of gunpowder, describing in eager detail how it can tear down walls, sink ships, and destroy a whole city. The king is horrified. He calls Gulliver an “impotent and grovelling insect” for sharing such ideas so cheerfully, and forbids him to ever mention it again.

Gulliver then describes Brobdingnag’s own government: short and simple laws, a learning focused on what is useful, and a king who values the farmer who grows more corn over the whole “race of politicians put together.”

⚡ TRY IT · WHOSE VALUE IS IT?

Each idea below comes from the chapter. Slide it toward the country that holds it, based on the reading. Decide in your head before you click.

1. “A new weapon that can destroy a whole city is a gift worth having.”
2. “Growing more food is more valuable than any clever politician.”
3. “The more books a country writes about ruling, the wiser it must be.”
4. “Laws should be so short and plain that no one can twist them.”
📚 PART 3 · READ & NOTICE: WHY REFUSE GUNPOWDER?

Gulliver thinks the king is foolish to turn down such power. He calls it “a strange effect of narrow principles.” But Swift may want us to read the moment the other way.

✏️ READING JOURNAL · ADD THIS TO THE WEEK 5 SECTION OF YOUR READING JOURNAL
Answer in 2 to 4 sentences, pointing to the king’s actual words in the chapter: Is the king foolish to refuse gunpowder, or wise? Then pick one row from the comparison table above and explain what Swift wants you to notice by setting England and Brobdingnag side by side.
⚠️ WORTH KNOWING · POWER WITHOUT JUDGMENT
Swift wrote almost three hundred years ago, but the gunpowder scene asks a question that is still live: when a new and powerful tool appears, should we use it just because we can? The king pauses to ask whether a thing is good, not only whether it works. That habit of asking is yours to build, no matter what tools you grow up with.

💪 BEFORE YOU GO

Swift never says outright which country is wiser. He puts the two beside each other and lets you compare. That is the reader’s real work: not to be told the answer, but to weigh the evidence and decide.
