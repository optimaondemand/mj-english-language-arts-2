Who Is Telling This Story, and Can We Trust Him?

📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A court official slips into Gulliver’s house at night and tells him the kingdom he saved has secretly charged him with treason. Every word of that scene reaches us through Gulliver. So how much should we believe?
You will read Chapter 7. You will learn what narrator and point of view mean, and you will weigh a real reader’s question: when one person tells the whole story, where might the account bend toward his side?
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to explain what a narrator is and identify the point of view in Gulliver’s Travels.
2
I will be able to tell the difference between a fact Gulliver reports and an opinion he slips in.
3
I will be able to judge how much to trust Gulliver’s account of the treason charges, and say why.

💡 THE ONE IDEA TO HOLD ONTO
A first-person narrator is not a window. He is a filter. Everything that happens has to pass through one person before it reaches you, and that person picks the words, leaves things out, and has feelings about it all.
🎭
What happened
The court votes to blind Gulliver as a mercy instead of killing him.
➞
👤
Gulliver, the filter
He chooses what to tell, what words to use, and how to make himself look.
➞
👀
What you receive
A wronged hero among cruel, ungrateful little people. That is his version.
📚 PART 1 · READ & NOTICE: BEFORE YOU READ
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
When someone tells you a story where they come off looking great, what do you do with it? (No wrong answer. Just check in with yourself.)
📚 PART 2 · READ & NOTICE: READ CHAPTER 7

Read Chapter 7 now. A friendly lord of the court comes to Gulliver in secret and shows him a paper: the Articles of Impeachment against Quinbus Flestrin, the Lilliputian name for Gulliver, the Man-Mountain. The admiral Skyresh Bolgolam and the treasurer Flimnap, both his enemies, have charged him with treason. The charges include putting out the palace fire in a way the law forbids, refusing to crush the rest of Blefuscu, and meeting with the Blefuscu ambassadors. The council debates his punishment. The kindest proposal, treated as great mercy, is to put out his eyes rather than execute him.

Watch one thing as you read: this whole scene is told by Gulliver, the very man on trial. He reports what the council decided, but he also tells you how to feel about it.

💡 TWO WORDS FOR YOUR TOOLKIT
A narrator is the voice telling the story. Point of view is the position that voice speaks from. This book is told in first person: the narrator is a character in the story and uses the word “I.” A first-person narrator can only show you what he saw and chose to share.
⚡ TRY IT · REPORTED FACT OR GULLIVER'S OPINION?

Each line below is something the reader meets in Chapter 7. Decide whether Gulliver is reporting a fact that another witness could confirm, or slipping in his own opinion about it. Decide in your head before you choose.

1. Bolgolam and Flimnap drew up articles of impeachment charging Gulliver with treason.
2. The charges against Gulliver are unfair, and the court is ungrateful after all he did.
3. The council’s gentlest proposal was to blind Gulliver rather than put him to death.
4. The emperor only pretends to be merciful, and is really as cruel as the rest.
⚡ TRY IT · HOW FAR CAN WE TRUST IT?

A reliable narrator is one the reader can trust to tell it straight. Gulliver is mostly honest about events, but he is on trial, so he has a reason to shape the telling. For each statement, choose how much weight a careful reader should give it.

1. Gulliver quotes, word for word, what the friendly lord whispered to him in private.
2. Gulliver was, in fact, charged and the court did debate what to do with him.
3. Bolgolam and Flimnap acted out of pure jealousy and hatred of Gulliver.
📚 PART 5 · READ & NOTICE: WHY TELL IT THIS WAY?

Swift could have written this story from the outside, with a narrator who sees everyone’s thoughts, including Bolgolam’s and the emperor’s. Instead he locked us inside Gulliver’s head. Think about what that choice does to the reader.

✏️ ADD THIS TO YOUR READING JOURNAL

Open your Reading Journal and go to Section 3, Week 3. Answer these three questions in 2 to 4 complete sentences each, pointing to a detail from Chapter 7 in every answer:

  1. List two of the charges in the articles of impeachment against Gulliver. Stick to what the text reports, not how Gulliver feels about it.
  2. The court calls blinding Gulliver an act of mercy. Find one word or phrase Gulliver uses that shows how he wants you to judge that mercy. What is his opinion doing to your view of the court?
  3. We only hear this trial from Gulliver. Pick one moment and explain how a Lilliputian official might describe that same moment differently. What does the gap tell you about trusting a single narrator?
⚠️ WORTH KNOWING · WHO IS NARRATING THE ANSWER?
An AI chatbot can summarize Chapter 7 in seconds, but its summary is its own kind of narrator. It picks what to include and what to leave out, just like Gulliver. The habit you practiced today, asking who is telling this and what they might be shaping, is exactly the habit you need when a machine hands you an answer.

💪 BEFORE YOU GO
