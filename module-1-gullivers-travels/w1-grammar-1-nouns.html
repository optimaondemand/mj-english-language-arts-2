Grammar 1.01
Nouns: Naming People, Places, Things, and Ideas
Lesson ≈12 min
📝 TODAY'S FOCUS
A noun names a person, place, thing, or idea. Today you will find the nouns in the first sentence of Gulliver’s Travels and learn the difference between common and proper nouns. You will be able to spot both kinds in your own reading and writing.
📚 THIS WEEK'S SENTENCE (from Chapter 1)
“My father had a small estate in Nottinghamshire; I was the third of five sons.”
⚡ WARM-UP
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Before the lesson, how comfortable do you feel naming the nouns in a sentence? (No wrong answer.)
💡 THE ONE RULE TO REMEMBER
Common noun
A general person, place, thing, or idea. Lowercase.
From the sentence: father, estate, sons
Proper noun
A specific, named person, place, thing, or idea. Always capitalized.
From the sentence: Nottinghamshire
📚 HOW TO FIND A NOUN: ASK QUESTIONS ABOUT THE SENTENCE

A noun names a person, place, thing, or idea. The surest way to find one is to ask questions about the sentence and let the answers point to the nouns. Watch how it works on this week’s sentence. First find the verb, then ask who or what the sentence is about.

  1. What is being said about my father?  He had. The word had is the verb (sometimes called the action word).
  2. Who or what had?  father. The answer is a noun, and because the sentence is about it, it is the subject noun.
  3. Father had a small what?  estate. The answer is a noun. The word a in front of it is an article adjective, not a noun.
  4. Where?  Nottinghamshire. It names one specific place, so it is a noun, and a proper noun, so it is capitalized. The word in before it is a preposition, and Nottinghamshire is the object of the preposition.
Label what you found:
My father
subject noun had
verb a
article adjective small
adjective estate
noun in
preposition Nottinghamshire.
proper noun

Two reminders: some nouns name things you cannot touch, like pride, honor, or freedom; those are ideas, and they still count as nouns. And the little words a, an, and the point to a noun but are not nouns themselves. They are called article adjectives because they sit in front of a noun.
