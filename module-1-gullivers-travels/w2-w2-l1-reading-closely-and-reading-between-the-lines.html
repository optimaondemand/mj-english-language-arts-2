📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
⌚ ~12 min
In Lilliput, the way to win a top government job is to jump highest on a tightrope. Swift never says “this is foolish.” So how do we know he thinks it is?
You will read Chapters 3 and 4. You will practice two kinds of reading: looking carefully at what the words actually say, and then figuring out what the author wants you to think but did not say out loud. The second kind is called making an inference.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to point to exact details in Chapters 3 and 4 that answer a question about the text.
2
I will be able to make an inference: a smart guess that the text supports but does not state outright.
3
I will be able to explain what the rope-dancing and the egg war in Lilliput suggest about real people.

💡 THE ONE IDEA TO HOLD ONTO
An inference sits one step above what the page says. You start with a detail you can point to, then add what you already know, and arrive at a conclusion the author left for you to reach.
TOP STEP · YOUR INFERENCE
Swift thinks the people who run governments often win power for silly reasons, not for being wise or good.
⬆️
MIDDLE STEP · WHAT YOU ALREADY KNOW
Jumping on a rope has nothing to do with being good at running a country.
⬆️
BOTTOM STEP · WHAT THE TEXT SAYS
“Whoever jumps the highest, without falling, succeeds in the office.”
📚 PART 1 · READ & NOTICE: BEFORE YOU READ
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
When a story does not tell you exactly what to think, how do you usually figure out the author’s opinion? (No wrong answer. Just check in with yourself.)
📚 PART 2 · READ & NOTICE: READ CHAPTERS 3 AND 4

Read Chapters 3 and 4 now. In Chapter 3, Gulliver wins more trust at court and watches the famous Lilliputian entertainments: ministers compete for office by rope-dancing, and the emperor hands out blue, red, and green silk threads to whoever can best leap over and creep under a stick. Gulliver builds a handkerchief stage for the cavalry, and the emperor has him stand like a giant while the army marches between his legs. Gulliver then earns his freedom by swearing to a long list of conditions.

In Chapter 4, Gulliver tours Mildendo, the capital, and the secretary Reldresal explains the empire’s two big troubles: a fierce split at home between the High Heels and the Low Heels over the height of a shoe, and a long, bloody war with the neighboring island of Blefuscu over whether to crack a boiled egg at the big end or the little end.

💡 KEEP THIS IN MIND
A detail is something the text states directly: you can underline it. An inference is something you build from the detail plus your own knowledge. A good inference can always be traced back to a detail you can point to.
⚡ TRY IT · DETAIL OR INFERENCE?

Each statement below comes from your reading. Decide whether it is a detail the text states directly, or an inference you would have to build. Decide in your head before you choose.

1. Flimnap, the treasurer, can jump higher on the rope than any other lord in the empire.
2. Choosing officials by rope-dancing is a risky and unwise way to run a country.
3. The Big-Endians and Little-Endians are fighting over something that does not really matter.
4. About eleven thousand people have died rather than crack their eggs the new way.
📚 PART 4 · READ & NOTICE: BUILD AN INFERENCE

The split between High Heels and Low Heels comes down to the height of a shoe. Two sides will not eat or talk with each other over it. Swift made the difference as small as he possibly could. Why?

✏️ READING JOURNAL · ADD THIS TO THE WEEK 2 SECTION

Write a few sentences for each question. For each one, point to a detail you can find in Chapter 3 or 4, then add your own inference.

  1. In Chapter 3, how do candidates win a high government office? Name the detail, then explain what it suggests about how Lilliput chooses its leaders.
  2. In Chapter 4, what started the war between Lilliput and Blefuscu? Describe the cause, then explain what Swift might want readers to think about real wars.
  3. The emperor calls himself “delight and terror of the universe,” yet he rules a kingdom about twelve miles across. What is the gap between his words and the truth, and what does that gap show?
⚠️ WORTH KNOWING · WHERE A TOOL STOPS AND YOU START
An AI chatbot can hand you a summary of what happens in Chapters 3 and 4. It cannot stand in for the move you just practiced: pointing to a detail and building your own inference from it. That skill is what lets you trust a conclusion, your own or anyone else’s. It matters more, not less, as the tools get better.
➡️ WHAT YOU'LL DO NEXT
1
Reading Journal: Week 2 entry
Answer the three reading questions above in the Week 2 section of your Reading Journal, pairing a detail with your inference each time. Your teacher reviews the Journal at the module checkpoints, not every week.
💪 BEFORE YOU GO

Swift rarely tells you what to think. He shows you a tiny world and trusts you to notice how much it looks like our own. Every time you reach an opinion about Lilliput, ask: which detail led me here?
