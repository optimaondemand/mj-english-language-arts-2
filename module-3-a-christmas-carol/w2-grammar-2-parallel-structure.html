Grammar 3.04
Parallel Structure: Items in a List That Match
Grammar ⌚ ~12 min
📖 TODAY’S FOCUS
When you list ideas in a sentence, each item should use the same grammatical form. Breaking the pattern creates a jarring sentence. Writers like Dickens use list structure deliberately to build rhythm and power.
💡 BALANCED LISTS VS. BROKEN LISTS
BALANCED (items match)
Scrooge laughed,
cried,
and danced.
Each item = past-tense verb
He became a good friend,
a good master,
and a good man.
Each item = “a good + noun”
BROKEN (items do not match)
Scrooge was laughing,
he cried,
and to dance.
Items mix: -ing, clause, infinitive
He became a good friend,
a good master,
and good.
Last item drops “a” and the noun
📚 READ & NOTICE: WARM-UP
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Listen to this sentence: “Scrooge was generous, kind, and he had joy.” Does something sound off to you?
📚 READ & NOTICE: THIS WEEK’S SENTENCE
THIS WEEK’S SENTENCE (from Stave 5; parallel and correct):
“He became as good a friend, as good a master, and as good a man, as the good old city knew.”

Work through it step by step:

1
What is being said about he? Answer: became. Became is the verb. It is a linking verb: it links the subject to what he became.
2
Who or what became? Answer: He. He is the subject pronoun.
3
What did he become? Three things: a friend, a master, a man. Each is introduced the same way: “as good a ___.” The three phrases balance. Each follows the same pattern.
CLASSIFIED SENTENCE
He became as good a friend, as good a master, and as good a man
subject pronoun linking verb parallel noun phrase 1 parallel noun phrase 2 parallel noun phrase 3

Matching items in a list is not just a grammar rule. It is a craft choice. “He became as good a friend, as good a master, and as good a man” feels complete and dignified because the three phrases balance. If the sentence ended “and good,” the final phrase breaks the pattern and the sentence loses its weight.
