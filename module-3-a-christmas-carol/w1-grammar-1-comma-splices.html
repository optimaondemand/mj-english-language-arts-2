Comma Splices: When a Comma Is Not Enough
Lesson ≈12 min
📝 TODAY’S FOCUS
Two complete sentences cannot be joined by a comma alone. Today you learn to spot that error, name it, and fix it three different ways. You will practice on sentences from A Christmas Carol.
📚 THIS WEEK’S SENTENCE (from Stave 1)
“Scrooge was his sole executor, his sole administrator, his sole assign, his sole residuary legatee, his sole friend, and his sole mourner.”
⚡ WARM-UP
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Before the lesson: how confident are you about when to use a comma versus a period? (No wrong answer.)
💡 THE ONE RULE TO REMEMBER
A comma cannot join two independent clauses.
An independent clause is a complete sentence: it has a subject noun or subject pronoun + a verb and can stand alone. Two of them side by side need more than a comma.
✗ COMMA SPLICE
Scrooge was cold, he never gave anyone a coin.
Two independent clauses. The comma is not enough.
THREE FIXES
1. Period: Scrooge was cold. He never gave anyone a coin.
2. Semicolon: Scrooge was cold; he never gave anyone a coin.
3. Coordinating conjunction after the comma: Scrooge was cold, and he never gave anyone a coin.
📚 HOW TO SPOT A COMMA SPLICE: FIND THE CLAUSES

To find a comma splice, ask yourself: can I split this sentence at the comma and have two complete sentences? If yes, the comma alone is wrong.

Watch the Q&A approach on a sentence invented to show the error:

Example sentence with a comma splice:
“The counting-house was cold, the clerk shivered at his desk.”
  1. What is being said about the counting-house? It was cold. Verb: was. Subject noun: counting-house. Complete sentence: yes.
  2. What is being said about the clerk? He shivered. Verb: shivered. Subject noun: clerk. Complete sentence: yes.
  3. Two independent clauses + only a comma = comma splice.
  4. Fix: “The counting-house was cold; the clerk shivered at his desk.”
