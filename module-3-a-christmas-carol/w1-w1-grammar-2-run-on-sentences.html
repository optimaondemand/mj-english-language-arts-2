Run-on Sentences: When Thoughts Keep Going and Going
Lesson ≈12 min
📝 TODAY’S FOCUS
A run-on sentence is what happens when two or more independent clauses collide without proper punctuation. Today you learn to find them, understand why they are different from comma splices, and fix them. You will practice on sentences drawn from Staves 1 and 2.
📚 THIS WEEK’S SENTENCE (from Stave 2)

💡 THE ONE RULE TO REMEMBER
A run-on is two independent clauses with NO punctuation between them at all.
A comma splice uses a comma but no conjunction. A run-on uses nothing. Both are errors. Both have the same fixes.
✗ RUN-ON
The ghost appeared Scrooge could not believe his eyes.
Two independent clauses. Nothing between them.
✗ COMMA SPLICE (for comparison)
The ghost appeared, Scrooge could not believe his eyes.
A comma alone is still not enough.
✓ THREE FIXES
1. Period: The ghost appeared. Scrooge could not believe his eyes.
2. Semicolon: The ghost appeared; Scrooge could not believe his eyes.
3. Conjunction: The ghost appeared, and Scrooge could not believe his eyes.
📚 FINDING THE CLAUSES IN THIS WEEK’S SENTENCE

Dickens’s real sentences are long and layered, but they are punctuated correctly. Watch the analysis below on a sentence built to show a run-on, using characters from the novella.

Example run-on sentence:
“The Spirit pointed its hand Scrooge followed in silence.”
  1. What is being said about the Spirit? It pointed. Verb: pointed. Subject noun: Spirit. Complete sentence: yes.
  2. What is being said about Scrooge? He followed. Verb: followed. Subject noun: Scrooge. Complete sentence: yes.
  3. Two independent clauses + nothing between them = run-on sentence.
  4. Fix with a period: “The Spirit pointed its hand. Scrooge followed in silence.”
Label what you found in the corrected first clause:
The
article adjective Spirit
subject noun pointed
action verb its
possessive pronoun hand
noun .

The period ends the first independent clause cleanly. The second clause then starts fresh as its own sentence.
