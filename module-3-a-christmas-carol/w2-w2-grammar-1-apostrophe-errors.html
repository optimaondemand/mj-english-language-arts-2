Apostrophe Errors: When to Use One and When to Leave It Out
Grammar ⌚ ~12 min
📖 TODAY’S FOCUS
An apostrophe has two jobs: it marks a possessive noun (the Spirit’s torch) and it marks a contraction (it’s = it is). Confusing these two jobs, or forgetting the apostrophe entirely, is one of the most common errors in writing. Today you will practice spotting the error, naming what went wrong, and fixing it. You will use sentences from A Christmas Carol.
💡 TWO JOBS FOR ONE MARK
JOB 1: POSSESSIVE NOUN
Shows ownership
the Spirit’s torch
Bob’s salary
Scrooge’s name
JOB 2: CONTRACTION
Shows letters left out
it’s = it is
can’t = cannot
didn’t = did not
COMMON TRAP
its = possessive pronoun, NO apostrophe: The Spirit opened its robe.
it’s = contraction for “it is,” HAS apostrophe: It’s cold outside.
📚 READ & NOTICE: WARM-UP
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Look at this sentence: “The Cratchits Christmas dinner was small but warm.” What is wrong with it? Take a guess before we work through it.
📚 READ & NOTICE: THIS WEEK’S SENTENCE
THIS WEEK’S SENTENCE (from Stave 3; contains an error):
“The Ghost of Christmas Presents torch lit the dark room.”

Work through it step by step:

1
What is being said about the Ghost of Christmas Present? Answer: lit. Lit is the verb.
2
Who or what lit? Answer: torch. The torch is the subject noun.
3
The torch belongs to the Ghost of Christmas Present. Present is a noun. When a noun shows ownership, add an apostrophe + s: Present’s.
4
Corrected sentence: “The Ghost of Christmas Present’s torch lit the dark room.”
CLASSIFIED SENTENCE
The Ghost of Christmas Present’s torch lit the dark room .

CONTRACTIONS
A contraction squeezes two words into one and uses an apostrophe to mark the missing letters. “It’s cold” means “It is cold.” But its (no apostrophe) is a possessive pronoun, like “The cat cleaned its paws.” Dickens uses both in A Christmas Carol, and mixing them up is an apostrophe error.
