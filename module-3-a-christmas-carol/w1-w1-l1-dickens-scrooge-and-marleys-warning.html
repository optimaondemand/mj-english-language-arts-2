Dickens, Scrooge, and Marley’s Warning

📖 TODAY’S FOCUS
A dead man’s ghost shows up dragging chains made of cash-boxes and ledgers. What could those chains possibly mean?
Today you meet Ebenezer Scrooge, the writer who invented him, and the ghost who sets the whole story in motion. You will read Stave 1 and pay close attention to how Dickens builds Scrooge’s character before any spirit ever arrives.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
Describe who Charles Dickens was and why he wrote a story about Christmas and poverty in 1843.
2
Describe Scrooge’s character using specific details from Stave 1.
3
Explain what Marley’s ghost and his chains represent as a warning to Scrooge.

💡 THE ONE IDEA TO HOLD ONTO
DICKENS SHOWS SCROOGE IN THREE WAYS
Everything we learn about Scrooge in Stave 1 is a deliberate choice by the author. Notice how each detail hardens our picture of who he is.
His body
“The cold within him froze his old features, nipped his pointed nose, shrivelled his cheek.”
His effect on others
Beggars ask for nothing; blind men’s dogs pull their owners away from him in the street.
His words
“Bah! Humbug!” to Christmas. “Let them die and decrease the surplus population.”

📚 PART 2 · READ & NOTICE: WHO WAS CHARLES DICKENS?

A writer who had known poverty himself

Charles Dickens was born in England in 1812. As a boy, his family fell into debt and he was sent to work in a factory pasting labels on bottles of shoe polish while his father sat in debtors’ prison. That experience never left him. When he became one of the most famous writers in the English-speaking world, he kept writing about what poverty did to children and families. He published A Christmas Carol in December 1843, and it sold out its first edition within days.

The story is divided into five sections called staves, a musical term Dickens chose deliberately: a stave is a group of lines in a piece of music. He wanted the whole novella to feel like a song, something that builds and resolves. This week you read Staves 1 and 2.

📅 HISTORICAL CONTEXT
Victorian England in 1843 was a world of sharp contrasts. Industrial factories produced great wealth, but working-class families, including many children, worked twelve-hour days in dangerous conditions. Dickens had read a government report on child labor just weeks before writing A Christmas Carol. Scrooge is not just one man; he stands for every person with the power to help who chooses not to.
📚 PART 3 · READ & NOTICE: NOW READ STAVE 1

Read Stave 1 now. Watch for how Dickens introduces Scrooge through specific, physical details: his cold office, the way he treats his clerk Bob Cratchit, his contemptuous response to his nephew Fred’s Christmas greeting, and his dismissal of two gentlemen collecting money for the poor. Then comes the night: Marley’s face appears in the door-knocker, and the ghost himself arrives, dragging a long, heavy chain.

Marley tells Scrooge that the chain was forged link by link from his own choices during his life: “I wear the chain I forged in life. I made it link by link, and yard by yard.” He warns that Scrooge’s own chain is just as long. Three spirits will come to give Scrooge a chance.

✏️ READING JOURNAL · WEEK 1
Open the Week 1 section of your Reading Journal and answer in 3 to 5 sentences: What three details from Stave 1 show you most clearly what kind of person Scrooge is? Name the details and explain what each one shows. Then: what does Marley mean when he says the chain was made “link by link”? Add your answers to the Week 1 section of your Reading Journal.

Reading Scrooge’s character

Now that you have read Stave 1, use the details Dickens gives you to sort what you know. An author builds a character’s personality through what the character says, what the character does, and how other people react. Decide in your head first, then answer.

⚡ TRY IT · WHAT DOES THIS DETAIL SHOW?
Each item below is from Stave 1. Decide which aspect of Scrooge’s character it reveals most directly.
1. Bob Cratchit tries to warm himself at the candle because Scrooge keeps the coal box locked.
2. Scrooge tells his nephew Fred that keeping Christmas is “humbug” and refuses the dinner invitation.
3. Marley’s ghost says: “I am here to-night to warn you, that you have yet a chance and hope of escaping my fate.”
