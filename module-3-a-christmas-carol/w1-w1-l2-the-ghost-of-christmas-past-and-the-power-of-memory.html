The Ghost of Christmas Past and the Power of Memory
Lesson ≈14 min
📖 TODAY’S FOCUS
The Spirit takes Scrooge back to places he has not seen for decades. He did not forget them. So why did they stop mattering?
Today you read Stave 2 and follow Scrooge through the memories the Ghost of Christmas Past forces him to witness. By the end of this lesson you will understand how Dickens uses memory as a tool of change.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
Describe what the Ghost of Christmas Past shows Scrooge and why each memory matters.
2
Explain how the memories in Stave 2 begin to affect Scrooge emotionally.
3
Identify the turning point in Stave 2 where Scrooge’s emotional response changes.

💡 THE STRUCTURE OF STAVE 2
🔔
Memory 1
Scrooge as a lonely schoolboy, left behind at Christmas while other children go home.
🎉
Memory 2
Young Scrooge at old Fezziwig’s party: a generous employer who knew how to make work a joy.
💔
Memory 3
Belle releases Scrooge from their engagement: “Another idol has displaced me; and if it can cheer and comfort you in time to come...I have no just cause to grieve.”
Each memory shows Scrooge something he chose to let go. The Spirit does not lecture. It simply shows.

⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Think of a memory that still affects how you feel about something today, even though it happened years ago. What kind of memory tends to stick with a person longest? (No wrong answer.)
📚 PART 2 · READ & NOTICE: NOW READ STAVE 2

Read Stave 2 now. The Ghost of Christmas Past appears as a strange, flickering figure, bright as a flame. It takes Scrooge through the air back to the scenes of his childhood and youth. Watch closely for the moments when Scrooge’s feelings change. He begins as a man who dismisses everything, but the memories catch him off guard.

Pay particular attention to the Fezziwig scene. Scrooge, watching it again, says softly: “He has the power to render us happy or unhappy; to make our service light or burdensome; a pleasure or a toil.” He is thinking about Fezziwig. He is also, without quite knowing it, thinking about himself and Bob Cratchit.

How memory works on Scrooge

Dickens does not have Scrooge give a speech about being sorry. Instead, he shows us Scrooge’s reaction in small physical details and unguarded words. Look for those moments as you answer the questions below. Think first, then click.

⚡ TRY IT · HOW DOES MEMORY AFFECT SCROOGE?
Each item below is drawn from Stave 2. Choose the best explanation for what that moment shows about how the memories are working on Scrooge.
1. Scrooge sees himself as a solitary child sitting alone at a school desk while the other children have gone home for Christmas.
2. After the Fezziwig party, Scrooge says: “I should like to be able to say a word or two to my clerk just now. That’s all.”
3. Belle tells young Scrooge: “Another idol has displaced me; and if it can cheer and comfort you in time to come, as I would have tried to do, I have no just cause to grieve.”
