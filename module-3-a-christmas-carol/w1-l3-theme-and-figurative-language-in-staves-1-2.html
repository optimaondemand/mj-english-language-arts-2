📖 TODAY’S FOCUS
Dickens does not just tell you that greed is dangerous. He shows you a ghost in chains and a counting-house so cold that the water in the pipes has frozen. What do those images say about the theme?
Today you name what A Christmas Carol is really about, and you look at how Dickens uses figurative language to carry that meaning. Then you write Quick Write 1.

1

💡 THEME VS. TOPIC
Topic
A one- or two-word label: greed, memory, generosity, redemption.
Example: “Christmas Carol is about greed.”
Theme
A complete statement about what the story says about that topic. It is the author’s argument.
Example: “Greed isolates a person and destroys every connection that could have made life worth living.”
📚 PART 1 · READ & NOTICE: FIGURATIVE LANGUAGE IN STAVES 1-2

Figurative language is language that means more than it literally says. Dickens uses it everywhere in this novella. Two figures are worth naming here:

  • Metaphor: a direct comparison without “like” or “as.” Marley’s chain is literally a chain. But when Dickens says it was forged “link by link” from cash-boxes and ledgers, the chain is a metaphor for a life spent in greed. The chain is the consequence made visible.
  • Personification: giving human qualities to a non-human thing. Dickens describes the cold of Scrooge’s office as something that has a will of its own: “The cold within him froze his old features.” The cold is inside Scrooge, not just outside him.
Before you answer, do this in your Reading Journal first:
Find one more example of figurative language in Staves 1 or 2 (other than the chain). Write the line, name the type (metaphor, simile, personification, or another), and explain what it adds to the meaning. Add this to the Week 1 section of your Reading Journal, then come back and check your thinking below.
⚡ TRY IT · WHAT KIND OF FIGURATIVE LANGUAGE IS THIS?

2. The Ghost of Christmas Past appeared “like a child: yet not so like a child as like an old man viewed through some supernatural medium.”
