Direct Objects: Who or What Receives the Action
Lesson
📝 TODAY'S FOCUS
⌚ ~12 min
A subject does an action. Often that action lands on someone or something. That someone or something is the direct object. Today you will find the direct object in a sentence from Chapter 1 using a short string of questions. Direct objects are how London tells you exactly what is being done, and to whom.
📚 THIS WEEK'S SENTENCE (from Chapter 1)
“Manuel doubled a rope around Buck’s neck.”
A shortened version of London’s line about the night Buck is led away.
⚡ WARM-UP
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
In the sentence above, Manuel does something. He doubles. What did he double? (No wrong answer. Say the word in your head.)
💡 THE ONE RULE TO REMEMBER
To find the direct object, ask three questions about the sentence, in order. The third question is the one that catches it.
QUESTION 1 · FIND THE VERB
What is being said about the subject? That word is the verb.
⬇️
QUESTION 2 · FIND THE SUBJECT
Who or what [verb]? That word is the subject noun.
⬇️
QUESTION 3 · FIND THE DIRECT OBJECT
[Subject] [verb] what or whom? That word is the direct object.
📚 HOW TO FIND THE DIRECT OBJECT: ASK THE QUESTIONS

Watch the three questions work on this week’s sentence.

  1. What is being said about Manuel?  Manuel doubled. Doubled is the verb, and it is an action verb.
  2. Who or what doubled?  Manuel. That noun is the subject noun.
  3. Manuel doubled what?  rope. The rope is the thing he doubled, so rope is the direct object.
Label what you found:
Manuel
subject noun doubled
verb a
article adjective rope
direct object around Buck’s neck.

Notice that the words after around are not the direct object. They tell where, not what Manuel doubled. A direct object always answers “what?” or “whom?” right after the action verb.
