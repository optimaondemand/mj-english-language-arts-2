📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
⌚ ~14 min
For five chapters Buck has been hardening into a creature of the wild. Then a man saves his life, and Buck feels real love for the first time. Does that love finish his transformation, or fight it?
You will read Chapter 6. As you read, you will track the two forces pulling on Buck at the same time: the deep love that ties him to John Thornton’s fire, and the wild call that keeps drawing him into the forest. Holding both forces at once is the key to understanding how Buck changes.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to describe how John Thornton’s love changes Buck, using details from Chapter 6.
2
I will be able to explain how love and the wild pull on Buck at the same time, and why that makes his transformation complicated.
3
I will be able to point to evidence that Buck is still wild even while he adores Thornton.

💡 THE ONE IDEA TO HOLD ONTO
In Chapter 6 two forces pull on Buck in opposite directions. London tells us plainly that the call lures him into the forest, but “the love for John Thornton drew him back to the fire again.” Buck stands in the middle of a tug-of-war.
🔥 THE FIRE · PULLS BUCK BACK
Love for John Thornton. Loyalty, the warm camp, sitting at a man’s feet. The “soft civilizing influence.”
↔
🌲 THE FOREST · PULLS BUCK AWAY
The wild call sounding in the woods. Instinct, the shades of old wolves, “the strain of the primitive.”
The love is the strongest force keeping Buck in the human world. That is exactly why it matters so much for the rest of the book.
📚 PART 1 · READ & NOTICE: BEFORE YOU READ
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Think of a bond strong enough to pull you back from something you really wanted to do. What keeps that bond strong? (No wrong answer. Just check in with yourself.)
📚 PART 2 · READ & NOTICE: READ CHAPTER 6

Read Chapter 6 now. John Thornton, the man who stopped Hal from beating Buck to death, nurses Buck back to health by the river. For the first time in his life Buck feels “love, genuine passionate love.” He had known a working partnership and a dignified friendship at Judge Miller’s home in California, but never this. Buck adores Thornton from a distance, lies for hours studying his face, and grows afraid that Thornton will leave him the way every other master has.

That love runs both ways and grows fierce. Buck attacks “Black” Burton when the man strikes Thornton. He plunges into killing rapids to drag Thornton to shore. He pulls a thousand-pound sled out of frozen snow when Thornton whispers, “As you love me, Buck.” Yet through it all London keeps reminding us that the wild is still alive in Buck. He still fights other dogs without mercy and still hears the call sounding “imperiously, deep in the forest.”

💡 KEEP THIS IN MIND
A counter-pull is a force pulling against the main direction of a story. Buck’s whole story so far has pulled him toward the wild. The love for Thornton is the counter-pull: the one thing strong enough to slow that change and keep him by the fire.
⚡ TRY IT · THE FIRE OR THE FOREST?

Each detail below is from Chapter 6. Decide whether it shows the pull of love and the fire, or the pull of the wild and the forest. Decide in your head before you choose.

1. Buck lies at Thornton’s feet for hours, studying every change in his face.
2. Buck shows no mercy to strange dogs and fights to the death over an advantage.
3. Buck feels compelled to turn from the fire and plunge into the forest, not knowing why.
4. Buck swims into deadly rapids again and again to save Thornton’s life.
📚 PART 4 · READ & NOTICE: WHY THE LOVE MATTERS

London could have written Buck’s change as a straight line away from civilization. Instead he gives Buck the deepest human bond of his life right when the wild is calling loudest. Ask yourself why a writer would slow the transformation down at exactly this moment.

✏️ READING JOURNAL · ADD THIS TO THE WEEK 5 SECTION

Answer in a few sentences each. Point to a detail from Chapter 6 each time.

  1. How is Buck’s love for Thornton different from anything he felt at Judge Miller’s home in California? Name one detail that shows the difference.
  2. Find one moment where Buck is still clearly wild, even though he loves Thornton. What does that moment tell you about his transformation?
  3. London writes that the call lured Buck into the forest, but “the love for John Thornton drew him back to the fire again.” In your own words, what is this sentence saying about the two forces inside Buck?
⚠️ WORTH KNOWING · WHERE A TOOL STOPS AND YOU START
An AI chatbot can tell you that Buck loves Thornton. It cannot decide for you what that love means for his transformation, or weigh it against the call of the wild. That judgment is yours to build from the details, and it is the exact thinking your essay will ask for.

Buck is most wild and most loving in the same chapter. London holds those two things side by side on purpose. Keep both in mind as you finish the book: the love is the one rope still tying Buck to the world of men.
