Grammar 2.02
Indirect Objects: To Whom or For Whom
Lesson
📝 TODAY'S FOCUS
⌚ ~12 min
Yesterday you found the direct object: the thing that receives the action. Sometimes a sentence also tells you who that thing is handed to or done for. That word is the indirect object. You will find it in a sentence from Chapter 1, after you find the direct object first.
📚 THIS WEEK'S SENTENCE (from Chapter 1)
“The man brought Buck water.”
Based on London’s line about the man in the red sweater, after the fight, bringing Buck water to drink.
⚡ WARM-UP
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
The man brought water. To whom did he bring it? Say the word in your head. (No wrong answer.)
💡 THE ONE RULE TO REMEMBER
Find the direct object first. Then ask one more question. If the sentence names who the action was done to or for, that word is the indirect object. The indirect object always sits between the verb and the direct object.
FIRST · FIND THE DIRECT OBJECT
[Subject] [verb] what or whom? That word is the direct object.
⬇️
THEN · FIND THE INDIRECT OBJECT
[Verb] [direct object] to whom or for whom? That word is the indirect object.
📚 HOW TO FIND THE INDIRECT OBJECT: ASK THE QUESTIONS

Watch all the questions work on this week’s sentence, in order.

  1. What is being said about the man?  The man brought. Brought is the verb, an action verb.
  2. Who or what brought?  man. That noun is the subject noun.
  3. The man brought what?  water. The water is the thing brought, so water is the direct object.
  4. The man brought water to whom?  Buck. The water was brought to Buck, so Buck is the indirect object.
Label what you found:
The
article adjective man
subject noun brought
verb Buck
indirect object water.
direct object

A quick test: if you can put to or for in front of a word and move it to the end (“The man brought water to Buck”), that word is the indirect object. You cannot have an indirect object without a direct object.

⚡ TRY IT · WHICH WORD IS THE INDIRECT OBJECT?
Each line has both a direct object and an indirect object. Find the direct object first, then ask “to whom or for whom?” and pick the indirect object. Decide in your head before you click.
1. “The express messengers gave Buck torment.”
2. “Manuel handed the stranger the rope.”
3. “The North taught Buck a hard lesson.”
4. “Perrault paid the man three hundred dollars.”
