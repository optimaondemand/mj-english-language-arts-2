Lesson 2.16 · The Final Chapter
The Sounding of the Call
What Buck’s change finally means
~13 min
📖 WHERE WE ARE IN THE BOOK
In Chapter 6 John Thornton saved Buck, and Buck loved him in return. That love was the one thing still tying Buck to the world of people. In this last chapter London tests that tie. By the end you will be able to name the theme the whole novel has been building toward and say what London argues about the pull between civilization and the wild. You will use that thinking all week as you write your final project.
📚 READ & NOTICE

Read all of Chapter 7, The Sounding of the Call, before you go on. It is short. As you read, watch the two forces pulling at Buck: John Thornton and the love that holds him in camp, and the wild brother and the howl in the forest that keep calling him away.

READ THE CHAPTER
Open Chapter 7 in your text. [Teacher: link the Chapter 7 PDF or e-text in Canvas Files here.] The novel is public domain, so you may read it freely.
WHILE YOU READ, ADD TO YOUR READING JOURNAL
Turn to the Week 6 section of your Reading Journal and answer these in your own words as you read:
  1. After the wolf becomes friendly and runs with Buck, Buck stops and turns back toward camp. What makes him turn around, and what does that tell you about what still holds him to the world of men?
  2. When Buck returns and finds the camp destroyed, the narrator says, “The last tie was broken. Man and the claims of man no longer bound him.” What is the “last tie,” and why does its breaking finally free Buck to answer the call?
  3. By the final page Buck runs at the head of the wolf pack. Does answering the call set Buck free, destroy who he was, or both? Point to one detail from the chapter that supports your view.
💡 THE TWO FORCES AT THE END
All module long Buck has been pulled between two worlds. In Chapter 7 the pull comes to a head, then resolves. Here is the contrast London sets up, and what tips the balance.
CIVILIZATION (the claims of man)
John Thornton
Buck’s love for Thornton keeps him in camp. After his first run with the wild brother, Buck turns back for him. While Thornton lives, Buck is half a dog of men.
⇆
THE WILD (the call)
The howl in the forest
The wild brother, the visions of the hairy man, and the “many-noted call” pull Buck away. Hunting the great moose, killing the Yeehats, he becomes a creature of the wild.
WHAT TIPS THE BALANCE
Thornton’s death removes the last claim of man. With nothing left to hold him, Buck answers the call, sits and howls with the pack, and runs into the woods as their leader.
📚 PUTTING THE WHOLE NOVEL TOGETHER

A theme is the idea about life that a whole story works out, not just one event. Across the module you watched Buck change in stages, and each stage built toward the ending. Reading the last chapter, you can now see the shape of the whole.

CHAPTERS 1 TO 2 · THE PAMPERED DOG IS BROKEN IN
Buck is stolen from a warm valley and learns the law of club and fang.
CHAPTERS 3 TO 5 · THE PRIMORDIAL BEAST AWAKENS
Buck beats Spitz, leads the team, and survives masters good and bad. The old instincts grow stronger.
CHAPTER 6 · THE COUNTER-PULL OF LOVE
John Thornton gives Buck a reason to stay. Love pulls against the wild.
CHAPTER 7 · THE CALL WINS
With Thornton gone, Buck answers the call and joins the pack. The transformation is complete.

London does not say in one sentence what it all means. That is your job as a reader. Some readers see freedom: Buck becomes fully himself, “running free in the open, the unpacked earth underfoot.” Others see loss: the loyal dog who loved Thornton is gone, replaced by a killer the Yeehats call the Evil Spirit. The novel holds both at once. Your job in the project is to argue which reading the text supports best.

📚 A WORD WORTH KNOWING

London uses the word primordial through the book, and it returns at the end. It means belonging to the earliest, most ancient times, before civilization. When Buck answers “the call of the younger world” and acts on instincts “from the old hunting days of the primordial world,” London means the deep, ancient part of Buck that civilization had covered over. Watch how the ending makes that ancient self the truest one.

📚 CHECK YOUR UNDERSTANDING

The narrator writes: “John Thornton was dead. The last tie was broken. Man and the claims of man no longer bound him.”

💡 THE LAST TIE
In your notebook, write what the “last tie” is in plain words. The last tie is Buck’s love for John Thornton.
⚡ TRY IT · WHAT DOES EACH DETAIL SUGGEST?

Below are details from Chapter 7. For each one, decide whether London makes Buck’s change feel mostly like freedom, mostly like loss, or like both at once. There is no single right reading for the chapter as a whole, but each detail leans a certain way. Decide first, then choose, and read the feedback.

1. After Thornton dies, the narrator says the loss “left a great void in him... a void which ached and ached, and which food could not fill.”
2. Running beside the wild brother, “Buck was wildly glad... running free in the open, the unpacked earth underfoot, the wide sky overhead.”
3. On the last page Buck “may be seen running at the head of the pack... leaping gigantic above his fellows... as he sings a song of the younger world.”
4. Avenging Thornton, Buck becomes “a live hurricane of fury,” and the Yeehats flee, “proclaiming... the advent of the Evil Spirit.”

Notice that the chapter gives you evidence for more than one reading. That is exactly what makes it a strong question to argue. Your essay will pick the reading you can defend best and use details like these as evidence.
