Lesson 2.05
Tone: How London's Word Choice Builds the Mood
Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
⌚ ~12 min
London could have written “Buck’s first day was busy.” Instead he wrote that it “was like a nightmare,” filled with “shock and surprise.” Same day. Very different feeling. That feeling is tone.
You will look again at Chapter 2 to study tone: the attitude an author builds through the exact words chosen. You will see how a few hard, cold word choices make the Northland feel brutal.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to define tone as the attitude an author builds through word choice.
2
I will be able to point to specific words in Chapter 2 that create a brutal, dangerous mood.
3
I will be able to swap a harsh word for a milder one and explain how the tone changes.
💡 THE ONE IDEA TO HOLD ONTO
Two writers can report the same event. The words they choose decide how it feels. London picks hard, violent words on purpose, so the reader feels the danger Buck feels.
A FLAT, NEUTRAL VERSION
“The other dog bit Curly’s face. The dogs gathered around. Soon she had died.”
Tells you what happened. You feel almost nothing.
LONDON'S VERSION
“Curly’s face was ripped open from eye to jaw... she was buried, screaming with agony, beneath the bristling mass of bodies.”
Same event. Now you feel the horror. The words do that.
📚 PART 1 · READ & NOTICE: WARM UP YOUR EAR
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
London calls the dogs “savages” and “wolfish creatures” instead of just “dogs.” What feeling does that word choice give you before anything even happens? (No wrong answer.)
📚 PART 2 · READ & NOTICE: HOW WORD CHOICE BUILDS TONE

Reread the opening of Chapter 2 and the death of Curly. Notice the kinds of words London reaches for: nightmare, shock, peril, savages, vicious, ripped, screaming, agony, bloody, trampled, lifeless. They are short, hard, and violent. Stacked together, they build one steady feeling: this place is brutal and unsafe.

The word an author picks for a thing is called diction. Diction is the main tool that builds tone. A “crowd” of dogs feels neutral. A “bristling mass of bodies” feels menacing. Both name the same dogs. Only the second one sets the tone.

💡 KEEP THIS IN MIND
Tone is the author’s attitude, the feeling the writing gives off. Diction is the word choice that creates it. To name a tone, point to the exact words that build it. A tone with no words behind it is just a guess.
⚡ TRY IT · WHICH WORD CARRIES THE TONE?

Each line is from Chapter 2. One word in each does the most to make the moment feel harsh or dangerous. Choose it. Decide in your head first.

1. “...every moment life and limb were in peril.”
2. “...Curly’s face was ripped open from eye to jaw.”
3. “...buried beneath the bristling mass of bodies.”
4. “Buck’s first day on the Dyea beach was like a nightmare.”
📚 PART 4 · READ & NOTICE: TEST THE TONE

Here is the best test of whether a word is carrying the tone: swap it for a milder one and see what happens to the feeling.

🔮 CLICK TO FLIP
London wrote that the dogs were “savages.” In your head, swap that word for “strangers.” How does the tone change? Decide before you flip.
💬 ONE WAY TO SEE IT
“Strangers” is just unfamiliar. The danger drains out. “Savages” says the dogs are wild and will hurt you. The plot is identical, but the brutal tone is gone with one word swapped. That proves the word, not the event, is what built the feeling.
✏️ READING JOURNAL · ADD THIS TO THE WEEK 2 SECTION

Answer each question in a few sentences. Quote the exact word or phrase you are talking about.

  1. In one word, how would you name the tone of Chapter 2 (for example: brutal, tense, cold). Then quote three words from the chapter that build that tone.
  2. Pick one of London’s harsh words and swap it for a milder one. Write both versions and explain how the feeling changes.
  3. Why might London want a brutal tone in this chapter? What does it help you understand about the world Buck must now survive?

💪 BEFORE YOU GO

The events of a story are only half of it. The other half is how the author makes them feel. From now on, when a passage hits you hard, slow down and ask which exact words did the hitting.
