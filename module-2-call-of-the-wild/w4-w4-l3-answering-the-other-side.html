Lesson 2.12 · Friday Workshop
Answering the Other Side
Short lesson
✏️ TODAY'S FOCUS
⌚ ~10 min
A strong argument does not pretend the other side does not exist. It names the strongest point against it, answers it honestly, and then lands on a firm conclusion.
You have built a claim, then evidence and explanation. This is the third step: the counterclaim and the conclusion. You will learn how to acknowledge the other side and answer it, then close your argument. In your Quick Write you will write a counterclaim and conclusion for your building argument about Buck.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to state a fair counterclaim: the strongest point someone could make against my claim.
2
I will be able to answer the counterclaim and show why my claim still holds.
3
I will be able to write a conclusion that restates my claim and leaves the reader with the main idea.
💡 THE ONE IDEA TO HOLD ONTO
Your argument is being built one step at a time. The first two steps are done. Today you add the last two pieces.
STEP 1 (DONE) · CLAIM
What you argue. (Week 2 Quick Write)
STEP 2 (DONE) · EVIDENCE + EXPLANATION
A detail from the text, plus how it supports your claim. (Week 3 Quick Write)
STEP 3a (TODAY) · COUNTERCLAIM AND ANSWER
Name the strongest point against you, then answer it: “Some might say… but…”
STEP 3b (TODAY) · CONCLUSION
Restate your claim in fresh words and leave the reader with your main point.
📚 WATCH ONE GET BUILT

Here is a worked example built on a claim about Chapters 4 and 5. Read how the counterclaim is named, answered, and then closed.

CLAIM: Survival on the trail depends on judgment, not on kindness.
EVIDENCE + EXPLANATION: The skilled drivers fed the dogs first and never overloaded the sled, and the team made a record run. Their judgment kept the dogs alive.
COUNTERCLAIM + ANSWER: Some might argue that Mercedes was the kindest owner, since she wept over the dogs and begged Hal not to whip them. But her kindness had no judgment behind it. She rode the sled while the dogs starved, so her feelings made things worse, not better.
CONCLUSION: Caring about the dogs was not enough. On the trail, it is knowing what to do that decides who survives.
⚡ TRY IT · IS THIS A FAIR COUNTERCLAIM?
A counterclaim should be the strongest fair point against your claim, stated honestly. For the claim “Survival on the trail depends on judgment, not kindness,” decide whether each statement is a fair counterclaim or not. Decide in your head before you click.
1. “Mercedes cared the most about the dogs, so feeling, not judgment, is what counts.”
2. “Some people think dogs do not need any food at all to pull a sled.”
3. “The skilled drivers were harsh and clubbed the dogs too, so maybe toughness is what really matters.”
4. “Judgment is clearly the most important thing for survival on the trail.”
🔍 ONE STEP FURTHER: SPOTTING FLAWED REASONING

You just decided whether a counterclaim was fair or not. That same skill works when you are reading anyone else's argument, not just building your own. Careful readers watch for the moment when a writer's or speaker's reasoning breaks down. Here are three common ways an argument goes wrong.

ATTACKING THE PERSON, NOT THE ARGUMENT
Criticizing the person who makes an argument instead of answering the argument itself. Example: “Don’t listen to Marcus about the class schedule, he’s always late to school.” Whether Marcus is late says nothing about whether his idea about the schedule is right.
A FALSE EITHER/OR
Presenting only two options as if they were the only possibilities, when more choices actually exist. Example: “Either we cancel recess completely, or the whole school stays out of control.” There are many options between those two extremes.
A HASTY GENERALIZATION
Drawing a broad conclusion from just one or two examples, without enough evidence to support it. Example: “My neighbor’s dog barked at me once, so every dog in this neighborhood must be aggressive.” One dog’s behavior does not prove a rule about all the others.

None of these examples come from The Call of the Wild. They are everyday arguments so you can practice noticing the pattern before you look for it in harder reading.

⚡ TRY IT · SPOT THE FLAWED REASONING
Read each short argument below. None of them come from the novel. Decide whether the reasoning is sound or flawed, and be ready to say why. Decide in your head before you click.
1. “Either everyone in class turns in their essay early, or the whole class deserves a lower grade for being lazy.”
2. “Three students in our class scored higher on the quiz after using the study guide, so the teacher plans to try it with the rest of the class next week to see if the pattern holds.”
3. “You shouldn’t trust Elena’s argument that the field trip should be rescheduled. She forgot her homework twice this month.”
🧰 SENTENCE FRAMES YOU CAN USE
To name the counterclaim: “Some readers might argue that…” / “It is true that…”
To answer it: “But…” / “However, the text shows…” / “Even so…”
To conclude: “In the end…” / “The chapter makes clear that…”
