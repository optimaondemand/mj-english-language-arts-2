When a Noun Does an Adjective’s Job
Lesson
📝 TODAY'S FOCUS
⌚ ~12 min
A word is usually a noun, but sometimes a noun stands in front of another noun and describes it: a river bank, a love name, a saw mill. When a noun answers “what kind” or “which one” about the noun beside it, it is doing an adjective’s job. London leans on this move to pack his Northland in tight, exact words.
📚 THIS WEEK'S SENTENCE (from Chapter 6)
“Buck slowly won back his strength by the river bank.”
A shortened version of London’s line about Buck’s recovery.
⚡ WARM-UP
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
In the phrase “river bank,” which word tells you what kind of bank it is? (No wrong answer.)
💡 THE ONE RULE TO REMEMBER
A noun is doing an adjective’s job when it sits in front of another noun and answers “what kind” or “which one.” The first noun describes; the second noun is the real thing being talked about.
🌊
river bank
What kind of bank? A river one.
❤️
love names
What kind of names? Love ones.
🦺
police dogs
Which dogs? The police ones.
Each first word is normally a noun. Here it has been put to work describing the noun beside it.
📚 HOW TO SPOT A NOUN DOING AN ADJECTIVE’S JOB

Work through this week’s sentence one question at a time. Find the verb and the subject first, then test the words near each noun.

  1. What is being said about Buck?  Buck won back. Won is the verb.
  2. Who or what won back?  Buck. That noun is the subject noun.
  3. Won back what?  strength. That is a noun.
  4. Won back where?  by the bank. Bank is a noun, and by is a preposition.
  5. What kind of bank?  a river bank. River is normally a noun, but here it answers “what kind,” so it is doing an adjective’s job.

The word river has not stopped being a noun. It is just standing in front of bank and describing it, which is an adjective’s job.
