📝 TODAY'S FOCUS
⌚ ~12 min
Some action verbs pass the action on to a receiver. Some do not. A transitive verb has a receiver: someone or something the action lands on. An intransitive verb has no receiver. Today you will test verbs from Chapter 2 with one simple question and sort them into the two kinds.
📚 THIS WEEK'S SENTENCE (from Chapter 2)
“Curly rushed her antagonist.”
London’s line from the fight that teaches Buck the law of club and fang.
⚡ WARM-UP
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
In the sentence above, the verb is rushed. After you say “Curly rushed,” does the sentence feel finished, or does your mind want to know one more thing? (No wrong answer.)
💡 THE ONE RULE TO REMEMBER
Find the action verb. Then ask the verb a question: the verb plus “whom?” or “what?” If the sentence gives an answer, the verb is transitive. If there is no answer, it is intransitive.
🎯
Transitive: has a receiver
“Curly rushed her antagonist.”
rushed whom? → antagonist. The action lands on a receiver.
❄️
Intransitive: no receiver
“Buck howled in the cold.”
howled whom or what? → no answer. “In the cold” tells where, not what he howled.
📚 HOW TO TELL THEM APART: ASK QUESTIONS ABOUT THE SENTENCE

Watch how the flow works on this week’s sentence.

  1. What is being said about Curly?  Curly rushed. Rushed is the action verb.
  2. Who or what rushed?  Curly. That noun is the subject noun.
  3. Rushed whom or what?  antagonist. There is a receiver, so rushed is a transitive verb. The receiver is the direct object.
  4. Compare a line with no receiver: “Buck howled.” Howled whom or what? Nothing answers. So howled is intransitive.
Label what you found:
Curly

direct object

The same verb can work both ways in different sentences. “Buck pulled the sled” is transitive (pulled what? the sled). “Buck pulled hard” is intransitive (pulled what? no answer; hard tells how). The test is always whether the sentence gives the verb a receiver.
