📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
⌚ ~12 min
A dog named Buck rules a sunny California estate without a worry in the world. By the end of this book he is the leader of a wolf pack in the frozen North. What turns one animal into the other?
You will meet the author, Jack London, and read the opening of Chapter 1, where you meet Buck in his old life. London believed that a creature is shaped powerfully by the place it lives. As you read, you will start watching how the setting of a story works on a character.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to describe Buck’s life and character at the start of the book, using details from the Santa Clara Valley pages.
2
I will be able to explain what the setting of a story is, and why where a character lives shapes who that character is.
3
I will be able to predict how a sudden change of setting might begin to change Buck.

🏛️ WHEN AND WHERE THIS WAS WRITTEN
Jack London published The Call of the Wild in 1903. A few years earlier, in 1897, gold was found in the Klondike, a region of the far Northwest, and thousands of people rushed north to dig for it. They needed strong dogs to haul sleds across the snow. London had gone north himself during that gold rush, so he wrote about a world he had seen with his own eyes: the cold, the hunger, the harshness, and the dogs caught up in it. He wrote in a plain, hard style that matched the world he was describing.
💡 THE ONE IDEA TO HOLD ONTO
The setting is the time and place a story happens. London believed a place does more than sit in the background. It presses on a creature and shapes what that creature becomes. Hold these two settings side by side, and you can predict the whole book.
☀️ THE OLD SETTING
Santa Clara Valley, California
Warm sun, wide lawns, a roaring library fire, full meals. Buck is safe, respected, and in charge.
➡️
❄️ THE NEW SETTING
The Northland, on the way to the Klondike
Cold, snow, hunger, rope, and the club. Nothing is safe, and nothing is given. Buck must change to live.
📚 PART 1 · READ & NOTICE: BEFORE YOU READ
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Think of a place where you feel completely comfortable and in charge. If you were suddenly dropped somewhere cold, strange, and unfriendly, what do you think would change about you first? (No wrong answer. Just check in with yourself.)
📚 PART 2 · READ & NOTICE: READ THE OPENING OF CHAPTER 1

Read the start of Chapter 1, up to the night Buck is led away. London opens with a warning: men have found gold in the North, and now they want big, strong, warm-coated dogs to pull their sleds. Then he shows you what Buck has to lose. Buck lives at Judge Miller’s place in the sun-kissed Santa Clara Valley, a huge estate with stables, orchards, and a swimming tank. He is four years old, weighs one hundred and forty pounds, and London calls him “king’” over the whole place. He swims with the Judge’s sons, escorts the daughters on walks, and lies by the library fire on winter nights.

London is careful to tell you that Buck is not soft. Hunting and outdoor life have kept his muscles hard. He has “a fine pride in himself.” That pride matters: it is the part of Buck the new world is about to test.

💡 KEEP THIS IN MIND
London does not waste the description of the estate. Every comfort he shows you, Buck is about to lose. When an author spends this much time on a setting, ask what the setting is doing to the character, not just where the character happens to be.
⚡ TRY IT · WHAT DOES THE TEXT SHOW US ABOUT BUCK?

Each statement is about Buck’s old life. Decide whether London tells it to you directly on the page, or whether it is a conclusion you have to build from the details. Decide in your head before you choose.

1. Buck weighs one hundred and forty pounds and has lived four years at Judge Miller’s place.
2. Buck has no experience with hunger, cold, or being treated cruelly.
3. The easy life at the estate has made Buck proud and used to being in charge.
4. Hunting and outdoor life have kept Buck’s body hard, not soft.
📚 PART 4 · READ & NOTICE: WRITE ABOUT WHAT YOU READ

London spends his first pages building a comfortable world so the loss of it will land harder. Use the questions below to capture Buck’s old life in your own words.

✏️ READING JOURNAL · ADD THIS TO THE WEEK 1 SECTION

Write a few sentences for each question. Point to a detail from the reading whenever you can.

  1. Describe Buck’s life at Judge Miller’s place. Name three details that show how comfortable and respected he is.
  2. London calls Buck “king” of the estate and says Buck has “a fine pride in himself.” What kind of dog is Buck because of where he lives?
  3. The very first sentence warns that “trouble was brewing.” Predict: when Buck is taken to a cold, harsh place, what is the first part of him you think will be tested?

Reading Journal: Week 1 entry
Answer the three questions above in the Week 1 section of your Reading Journal. Your teacher reviews the Journal at the module checkpoints, not every week.
💪 BEFORE YOU GO

Right now Buck is a comfortable king. Keep that picture clear, because the rest of the book is the story of what the cold North does to it. The setting is not just scenery here. It is the force that will remake the dog.
