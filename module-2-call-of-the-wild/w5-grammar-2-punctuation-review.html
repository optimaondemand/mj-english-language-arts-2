Punctuation: Marks That Keep Sentences Clear
Lesson
📝 TODAY'S FOCUS
⌚ ~12 min
Punctuation marks are road signs. A comma asks the reader to pause; a semicolon joins two complete sentences that belong together; a colon says “here it comes.” You have been writing arguments all module, so clean punctuation matters: it keeps your claim and your evidence easy to follow. Today you will review four marks using sentences from Chapter 6.
📚 THIS WEEK'S SENTENCE (from Chapter 6)
“This man had saved his life, which was something; but, further, he was the ideal master.”
London’s line about why Buck loves John Thornton.
⚡ WARM-UP
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Read this week’s sentence aloud. Where do you naturally pause the longest? (No wrong answer.)
💡 THE FOUR MARKS TO KNOW
,
Comma — a short pause. Use it between items in a list, after an introductory part, and to set off an interrupting piece.
;
Semicolon — joins two complete sentences (two independent clauses) that are closely related, without a conjunction.
:
Colon — says “here it comes.” Use it after a complete sentence to introduce a list, an example, or an explanation.
. ? !
End marks — close a sentence. A period for a statement, a question mark for a question, an exclamation point for a strong feeling or command.
📚 HOW THE MARKS WORK IN THIS WEEK’S SENTENCE

Look again at the sentence from Chapter 6. The trick to punctuating it is to find the complete sentences inside it. A complete sentence has a subject and a verb and can stand alone.

  1. First complete sentence: “This man had saved his life.” The subject noun is man; the verb is had saved.
  2. Second complete sentence: “He was the ideal master.” The subject pronoun is he; the verb is was.
  3. Both could stand alone, and they are closely related, so London joins them with a semicolon.
  4. The small added pieces, “which was something” and “further,” are set off with commas because they interrupt the main idea.
Label what each mark is doing:
This man had saved his life
complete sentence ,
comma which was something
added piece ;
semicolon but, further,
commas set off “further” he was the ideal master
complete sentence .
end mark

The semicolon is what tells you the two halves are each a full sentence. If London had used a comma there instead, it would be a run-on error: two sentences glued with only a comma.
