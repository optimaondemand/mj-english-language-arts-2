📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
⌚ ~12 min
London does not just say the snow was cold. He says the wind “cut like a white-hot knife” and the rabbit fled “like some pale frost wraith.” The frozen world is not a backdrop. It feels alive.
You will finish Chapter 3, the fight to the death between Buck and Spitz. Then you will study three tools London uses to make the wild feel alive: comparing one thing to another, giving the land human qualities, and choosing words that hit hard.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to tell a simile from a metaphor and from personification in London’s writing.
2
I will be able to find figurative language in Chapter 3 and explain the feeling it creates.
3
I will be able to explain how London’s word choices make the wild feel like a living force, not just scenery.

💡 THREE TOOLS THAT BRING THE WILD TO LIFE
Figurative language means words that mean more than their plain, literal sense. London leans on three kinds. Each example below is from Chapter 3.
🪟
Simile
Compares two things using like or as.
“a wind that cut like a white-hot knife”
🔥
Metaphor
Says one thing is another, with no like or as.
“the dominant primordial beast” in Buck
🌲
Personification
Gives a thing or idea human qualities.
the cry of Life plunging down from Life’s apex
📚 PART 1 · READ & NOTICE: BEFORE YOU READ
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
A writer could say “the rabbit ran fast” or could say the rabbit fled “like some pale frost wraith.” Why might London pick the second one? (No wrong answer.)
📚 PART 2 · READ & NOTICE: FINISH CHAPTER 3

Read from the rabbit chase to the end of the chapter. The team chases a snowshoe rabbit by moonlight, and Buck feels “the blood lust, the joy to kill.” Spitz cuts across the bend and kills the rabbit first. Buck drives in, and the long war ends in a fight to the death, watched by a silent circle of sixty dogs. Buck wins by using his head as well as his teeth, and he stands at the end as “the dominant primordial beast who had made his kill and found it good.”

💡 KEEP THIS IN MIND
As you read, notice the words London capitalizes, like Life, Death, and Time. He treats them like characters with power. That is personification, and it is one way he makes the wild feel like a living force pulling at Buck.
⚡ TRY IT · NAME THE TOOL

Each line below is from Chapter 3. Decide which tool London is using. Think first, then choose.

1. “Twice his teeth clipped together, like the steel jaws of a trap.”
2. “Mercy was a thing reserved for gentler climes.”
3. Buck chases “like some pale frost wraith,” and the rabbit is “a frost wraith” too.
4. “the cry of Life plunging down from Life’s apex in the grip of Death.”
📚 PART 4 · READ & NOTICE: FROM TOOL TO FEELING

Naming the tool is only step one. The real work is explaining the feeling it creates. Take the simile “a wind that cut like a white-hot knife.” In your notebook, write one sentence: what does that comparison make the cold feel like? A knife cuts and a white-hot one burns, so the comparison makes the cold feel sharp and painful at the same time. The wind is not just unpleasant. It is an attacker. That is how London turns weather into one more enemy Buck has to survive.

✏️ READING JOURNAL · ADD THIS TO THE WEEK 3 SECTION

Write a few sentences for each question. Point to the exact words from Chapter 3 each time.

  1. Find one example of figurative language in the fight scene. Quote it, name the tool, and explain the feeling it creates.
  2. London keeps comparing Buck and the other dogs to wolves and ghosts (“ill-tamed wolves,” “frost wraith”). What does that pattern suggest about where Buck is heading?

💪 BEFORE YOU GO

London could have written a plain report of a dog fight. Instead he made the snow, the cold, and even Death feel alive. That choice is why the scene grips you. When you write your own evidence this week, the right quote will do the same work: it will make your point feel alive.
