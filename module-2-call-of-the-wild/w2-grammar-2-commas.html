Commas: Three Jobs That Keep a Sentence Clear

📝 TODAY'S FOCUS
⌚ ~12 min
A comma tells the reader where to pause and how the parts of a sentence fit together. Today you will learn three of its most common jobs, using sentences from Chapter 2. Get these three right and your writing reads clearly the first time.

💡 THE THREE JOBS TO REMEMBER
Each card below shows one job a comma does, with a line from Chapter 2.
📝
1. Items in a series
Three or more items: comma after each, including before the last “and.”
“...to stop, to go ahead, and to swing wide.”
⏩
2. After an opener
A word or phrase before the main sentence gets a comma after it.
“By evening, Perrault secured another dog.”
⦻
3. Around an interrupter
Extra info you could lift out gets a comma on each side.
“Spitz, the lead dog, ran out his tongue.”
📚 HOW THE SERIES COMMA WORKS

Look at this week’s sentence. Buck learns three things. Each one is an item in the series, so each gets a comma, and a comma also comes before the final “and.”

Mark the series:
to stop at “ho,”
item 1 , to go ahead at “mush,”
item 2 , and
conjunction to swing wide.
item 3

The little word and here is a conjunction, joining the last item to the rest. The comma before it is the one writers forget most often. Put it in, and a three-item list always reads cleanly.
