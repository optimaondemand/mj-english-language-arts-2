Grammar 2.08
Correlative Conjunctions: Words That Work in Pairs
Lesson
📝 TODAY'S FOCUS
⌚ ~12 min
A conjunction joins words or groups of words. Some conjunctions come in pairs, and the two parts always travel together: either…or, neither…nor, both…and, not only…but also, whether…or. These are correlative conjunctions. Today you will find a pair in a real sentence from Chapter 5 and learn the rule that keeps them balanced.
📚 THIS WEEK'S SENTENCE (from Chapter 5)
“Not only did they not know how to work dogs, but they did not know how to work themselves.”
London’s line about Hal, Charles, and Mercedes.
⚡ WARM-UP
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
In this week’s sentence, two little words frame the two failings of the owners. Which pair do you think is doing that work? (No wrong answer. Just look.)
💡 THE ONE RULE TO REMEMBER
Correlative conjunctions always come in pairs, and both halves must appear. Memorize the common pairs as set phrases.
either … or
neither … nor
both … and
not only … but also
whether … or
The two things the pair joins should match in form. “Neither the dogs nor the people were aware of it” joins two nouns. Keeping both sides parallel is what makes the sentence balanced.
📚 HOW TO FIND THE PAIR: ASK QUESTIONS ABOUT THE SENTENCE

Watch how it works on this week’s sentence.

  1. Is there a word that sets up a list of two?  Yes: not only. That is the first half of a pair.
  2. Where is its partner?  but (here the “also” is left out, which is allowed). Together, not only…but is the correlative conjunction.
  3. What does the pair join?  Two failings: not knowing how to work dogs, and not knowing how to work themselves.
Label the week’s sentence:
Not only
conjunction (pair, part 1) did they not know how to work dogs,
first idea joined but
conjunction (pair, part 2) they did not know how to work themselves.
second idea joined

A common slip is starting with one half and forgetting the partner. If you write “either,” your reader is waiting for “or.” Both halves must show up.
