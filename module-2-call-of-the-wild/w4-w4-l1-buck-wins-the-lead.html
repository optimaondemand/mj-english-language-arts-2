💡 THE ONE IDEA TO HOLD ONTO
Buck does not just take the lead. He takes it, then earns it. The chapter title asks who has “won to mastership,” and the answer comes in two steps: first the claim, then the proof.
STEP 2 · HE PROVES IT
Buck does what Spitz never could. He makes the loafer Pike pull, punishes the sour Joe, and breaks in new dogs so fast it takes the driver’s breath away. The team becomes one again.
⬆️
STEP 1 · HE CLAIMS IT
Buck will not take any place but the front. He circles the camp for an hour, dodging clubs, until the men give in. He wants the leadership. It is his by right.
📚 PART 1 · READ & NOTICE: BEFORE YOU READ
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
When you think someone has truly earned a leadership role, what convinces you? (No wrong answer. Just check in with yourself.)
📚 PART 2 · READ & NOTICE: READ CHAPTER 4

Read Chapter 4 now. The morning after the fight with Spitz, Buck refuses every spot but the lead. François tries Sol-leks; Buck drives him out. The men chase Buck with clubs for nearly an hour, but he will not take a lesser place. When they finally throw down the club and call him to the front, he trots in “laughing triumphantly.” On the trail he proves the best leader the drivers have ever seen, and the team makes a record run to Skaguay.

Then Perrault and François hand the team to a Scotch half-breed driving the mail. The work turns to heavy, monotonous toil. One dog, Dave, falls sick. When the driver pulls him out of the harness to rest him, Dave fights to get back in. He would rather die in the traces than be replaced, because “the pride of trace and trail was his.”

💡 KEEP THIS IN MIND
London ties two ideas together in this chapter: leadership (who gets to be in front and command the team) and mastery (being so good at the work that others fall into line). Buck wins both. Dave already has them, and will not give them up even when they kill him.
⚡ TRY IT · CLAIMING OR PROVING?

Each detail below is from Chapter 4. Decide whether it shows Buck claiming the lead (demanding it) or proving the lead (earning it through results). Decide in your head before you choose.

1. Buck springs on Sol-leks “in a fury,” driving him back and standing in his place.
2. By the end of the first day, Pike, who had always loafed, was pulling more than ever in his life.
3. Buck circles the camp for nearly an hour, dodging thrown clubs, refusing to take any place but the lead.
4. The speed with which Buck broke in the new dogs Teek and Koona “took away François’s breath.”
📚 PART 4 · READ & NOTICE: DAVE AND THE PRIDE OF THE TRAIL

Dave is dying. The driver does the kind thing and takes him out of the harness so he can rest. But Dave fights to get back in. He bites through Sol-leks’s traces and stands in his own place. London writes that “the pride of trace and trail was his.” The drivers decide to let him die in harness, “heart-easy and content.”

Think it through before you write:
In your notebook, answer first: Why would a sick dog fight to keep doing the work that is killing him? Then click to compare.
For Dave, the work is who he is. His place in the traces gives him pride and purpose. Being cut out feels worse to him than the pain of pulling. That is the same drive that makes Buck demand the lead.

⚠️ WORTH KNOWING · WHERE A TOOL STOPS AND YOU START
An AI tool can tell you that Buck becomes the lead dog. It cannot decide for you what that means: whether Buck is becoming a stronger creature or losing the gentle house dog he used to be. That judgment is the reading work, and it is yours. Build it on the details, and you will be able to defend it.
